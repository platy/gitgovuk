Building Safety Fund for new applications (2022): A guide for leaseholders and residents

The Building Safety Fund (BSF) provides funding to address life safety fire risks associated with cladding in high-rise buildings (those over 18m) in England. This is a guide on how the adapted 2022 BSF process works.

The process is slightly different for social housing applications. For more information, please refer to the main guidance.

This glossary of terms helps you understand the terms used in the guidance.

Application submitted

An application to the BSF can be made by the organisation that has the legal obligation or right to carry out remediation work on their building. In the guidance, these applicants are referred to as ‘responsible entities’.  They may be the building’s:

  • freeholder,
  • head leaseholder,
  • Resident Management Company (RMC)
  • Right To Manage (RTM) company or
  • social housing provider.

A responsible entity can authorise a managing agent to act on their behalf, apply to the BSF and manage the application on a day-to-day basis.

All applicants submit an application form, along with supporting evidence, including:

  • a Fire Risk Appraisal of External Wall construction (FRAEW), and
  • evidence of the height of the building.

Private registered providers of social housing are required to submit additional documentation, including:

  • confirmation that the works are not affordable, and
  • Vendor Form SAP7B (a form used by the Department for Levelling Up, Housing and Communities to enable payment to their suppliers/creditors).

Details on supporting documents and eligibility criteria can be found in the guidance.

Eligibility decision

The Department for Levelling Up, Housing and Communities reviews the application against the BSF eligibility criteria, identifying any issues that prevent the application from progressing.

The applicant may be asked to revise and resubmit documentation if it does not meet the requirements set out in the guidance.

When eligibility is confirmed, the application is passed to the Department’s delivery partners (either Homes England or The Greater London Authority) to progress.

If your building is not eligible, your responsible entity should tell you, providing details on request.

Legal checks and pre-tender support

The delivery partner makes legal checks to:

  • ensure that funding goes to the legitimate individual/organisation, and
  • verify the presence of any qualifying leaseholder(s) in the building.

Further information may be requested from the applicant.

If a private sector applicant needs help to produce a BSF tender, they can apply for pre-tender support. This is an advance payment, available to those who do not have the finances to start the project.

Cost assessment

The applicant hires competent professionals and contractors to scope out and deliver remediation works.

They send a detailed plan of the works and costs to their delivery partner who checks that costs are reasonable and in scope.

Any subsequent changes to costs, requiring additional funding, must be approved by the Department for Levelling Up, Housing and Communities. Cost variations could cause delays.

Funding agreement

If the application is approved by the Department for Levelling Up, Housing and Communities, the applicant signs a legal agreement covering the terms and conditions for funding called a Grant Funding Agreement.

Works start

The applicant is required to enter a works contract with the main contractor and must confirm:

  • a start date, and
  • an estimated completion date.

Applicants are required to send monthly project updates to their delivery partner to make sure the project stays on track.

Works complete

This is called practical completion. The applicant ensures that competent professionals, such as engineers, architects and quantity surveyors, sign off the works. They provide the delivery partner with proof of this.

They must also get the building’s Fire Risk Appraisal of External Wall construction (FRAEW) updated to consider the completed works.
