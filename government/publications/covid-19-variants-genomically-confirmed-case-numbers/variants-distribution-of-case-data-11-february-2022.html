Variants of concern or under investigation: data up to 9 February 2022

Surveillance overview

From 31 January 2022, the UK Health Security Agency (UKHSA) will report episodes of infection in its coronavirus (COVID-19) surveillance. Current surveillance of COVID-19 in England is based on positive samples from the same person grouped together, so that the person is only counted once and reported using the date of the first positive result. From 31 January 2022, an ‘episode’ definition will be introduced, grouping positive results up to and including 90 days apart into episodes. Positive test results which are more than 90 days apart will be classed as separate episodes.

As part of this change, the production of the national Variants and Mutations (VAM) line list has been integrated into the main COVID-19 pipeline, and several enhancements made to VAM data, including new fields and changes to the type of VAM result available. This brief document summarises what data were available on the old person-level VAM line list and how this is changing. It also outlines changes being made to some of the critical fields used for VAM reporting and provides guidance on how these should be interpreted and used.

Please be advised that the table column ‘New cases since last update’ may not be completely accurate. This is due to a comparison between the current (episode level) linelist and the previous (case level) linelist. This should be corrected in the next report.

Differences between a variant of concern and a variant under investigation

SARS-CoV-2 variants, if considered to have concerning epidemiological, immunological or pathogenic properties, are raised for formal investigation. At this point they are designated variant under investigation (VUI) with a year, month, and number. Following a risk assessment with the relevant expert committee, they may be designated variant of concern (VOC).

Changes in nomenclature

The previous variant names frequently include similar numbers with similar or repeating patterns. This makes referring to them verbally challenging. To overcome this challenge, a revised nomenclature has been agreed involving dropping the first 2 numbers of the year and replacing the 2-month numbers with a 3-letter abbreviation. This shortens the name and reduces the degree of repeated sequences of numbers. The table containing surveillance overview ‘UK total distribution’ contains a list of new variant names alongside previous or other names in use for VOCs and VUIs.
