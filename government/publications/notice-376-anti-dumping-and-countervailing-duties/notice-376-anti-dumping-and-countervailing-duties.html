1. Overview

1.1 What is this notice about

This notice explains:

  • what anti-dumping duty and countervailing duty are
  • how they’re administered
  • how to claim refunds of anti-dumping and countervailing duties, where applicable

If you believe that a product is being dumped or is receiving a countervailable subsidy, you can ask the Trade Remedies Authority (TRA) to investigate. You’ll need to register for the Trade Remedies service and complete an application for a dumping, subsidy or safeguard investigation. For help with this email: contact@traderemedies.gov.uk.

1.2 Laws that cover this notice

UK law:

  • primary legislation in the Taxation (Cross-border Trade) Act 2018 (the TCTA) Schedule 4 to the TCTA describes how dumping investigations are to be conducted
  • Secondary legislation in the Trade Remedies (Dumping and Subsidisation) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019 (the Dumping and Subsidisation)
  • Part 2 of the Dumping and Subsidisation Regs covers how the amount of dumping is determined
  • Part 3 of the regulations covers how the amount of countervailable subsidy is determined
  • Regulation 128 of the Customs (Import Duty) (EU Exit) Regulations 2018 (a) gives a definition of related persons — we use this to determine association
  • World Trade Organization (WTO) — relevant provisions
  • Article 2 of the Anti-Dumping Agreement provides guidance on calculating dumping margins
  • UK Taxation or trade remedies notices will govern each individual anti-dumping duty measure — they give information about when the measures take effect

2. Anti-Dumping Duty

2.1 What anti-dumping duty is

This is a duty on imports. It gives protection against the dumping of goods in the UK at prices much lower than the normal value, and damage to UK industry.

The reference price for ‘normal’ value is usually determined by the price the foreign producer charges for comparable sales in their own country. Each anti-dumping duty measure covers specified goods, originating in or exported from, named countries or exporters.

With the exception of when safeguard duties also apply, it’s charged in addition to, and independent of, most other duties to which the imported goods are liable. For example, third country import duty, or rebalancing duties.

Where a safeguard duty is also applied to an import, the anti-dumping duty will be amended so that only the highest of either the safeguard or anti-dumping duties apply.

The following examples show how the duties will be applied:

Example 1

An import is liable to 25% safeguard duty and 35% anti-dumping duty rate.

The highest rate of import duty for the goods is anti-dumping duty at 35%, so the highest rate of duty applies. However, because the law says safeguard duty (at 25%) must be taken first, the anti-dumping duty will be lower:

35% – 25% = 10%

Only 10% of the anti-dumping duty and 25% safeguard duty will be taken which will make a total of 35%.

Example 2

An import is liable to 25% safeguard duty.

It also has 9% anti-dumping duty and 11% countervailing duty, so the combined rate is 20%.

The highest rate of import duty for the goods is safeguard duty at 25%, so the anti-dumping and countervailing duties are reduced to zero and only the 25% safeguard duty rate applies.

2.2 Provisional anti-dumping duty

Provisional anti-dumping duty may be imposed during the investigation of an alleged case of dumping. It may be imposed where there is evidence dumped goods have been imported, or are being imported into the UK and are causing, or have caused injury to UK industry.

When provisional anti-dumping duty is imposed, as an importer you must provide a security to cover the provisional anti-dumping duty amount you will incur if the measures are made definitive at the end of the investigation.

At the end of the investigation, the provisional anti-dumping duty may be made definitive, may lapse, or be cancelled.

You must secure provisional anti-dumping duty by one of the following:

  • cash deposit
  • bond
  • bank guarantee

The security is not charged until the final duty is imposed.

If the definitive anti-dumping duty rate is less than the provisional duty rate and you’re entitled to repayment of provisional anti-dumping duty, you need to submit repayment claim on form C285.

Send your completed form to the National Import Duty Adjustment Centre by email to: customsaccountingrepayments@hmrc.gov.uk.

Claims must include a copy of the original import declaration and commercial documents such as invoices and transport documents. If you’re a trader and you’ve claimed a company specific rate of duty, you must provide documentation to support your claim.

Include your bank details with the claim so that a direct credit can be made to your account. This will also make sure you get a quicker and more secure repayment.

Security will be held for 4 months from the final ruling date. After this time, you should send claims by email to the National Duty Repayment Centre: ndrc.nch@hmrc.gov.uk.

3. Countervailing duty

3.1 What countervailing duty is

This is a duty imposed on imported goods which have received countervailable subsidies in the originating or exporting country. It’s treated in the same way as anti-dumping duty for customs purposes — see section 2.1 in this notice. It’s possible to have both anti-dumping duty and countervailing duty on the same product.

3.2 Provisional countervailing duty

A provisional countervailing duty can be imposed during a trade remedies investigation, where there is evidence that subsidised goods have been, or are being imported into the UK and are causing or have caused injury to UK industry.

When a provisional duty is imposed, as an importer you must provide a security for payment to cover the provisional countervailing duty amount, you will incur if the measures are made definitive.

At the end of the investigation, the provisional countervailing duty may be made definitive or cancelled.

You must secure provisional countervailing duty by one of the following:

  • cash deposit
  • bond
  • bank guarantee

The security is not brought to account until the definitive duty is imposed.

If the definitive countervailing duty rate is less than the provisional duty rate and you’re entitled to repayment of provisional anti-dumping duty, you need to submit repayment claim on form C285.

Send your completed form to the National Import Duty Adjustment Centre by email to: customsaccountingrepayments@hmrc.gov.uk.

Claims must include a copy of the original import declaration and commercial documents such as invoices and transport documents. If you’re a trader and you’ve claimed a company specific rate of duty, you must provide documentation to support your claim.

Include your bank details with the claim so that a direct credit can be made to your account. This will also make sure you get a quicker and more secure repayment.

Security will be held for 4 months from the final ruling date. After this time, you should send claims by email to the National Duty Repayment Centre: ndrc.nch@hmrc.gov.uk.

4. Registration

There are different types of registration, each has its own conditions in place to make sure that duty is protected during a Trade Remedies Authority investigation.

Registration makes sure imported goods, subject to an anti-dumping or countervailing duty investigation, are recorded and the appropriate charges are collected, following clearance.

Importers of goods subject to registration are told by HMRC and by public notice on gov.uk. The UK Global tariff also shows the commodity codes subject to registration.

5. Circumvention

Circumvention is when anti-dumping and countervailing duties are imposed on products either manufactured in, or exported from (or both), the country of non-preferential origin. Some traders try avoiding these duties by sending their goods to a different country to undergo processes that are not economically justified. For example, goods may be sent to another country to be re-packaged, then exported to the UK, to make it seem like the goods came from the re-packaging country.

Where this type of circumvention is identified, either anti-dumping or countervailing measures (or both) may be extended to imports of affected goods from the ‘transit’ country. This is regardless of their country of origin.

Another example of circumvention activity is the unnecessary alteration of product specifications to exclude them from the anti-dumping commodity codes.

6. How the system works

6.1 How is an investigation of alleged dumping started

Find out about the UK trade remedies investigations process.

6.2 Country of export

Anti-dumping and countervailing duty measures are imposed on the country of non-preferential origin. Where circumvention has been determined the duty may be extended to goods consigned from named countries whether they originated there or not.

The term ‘consigned from’ is broader than ‘the country from where the goods were shipped’. Exporters not involved in circumvention can apply to the Trade Remedies Authority for exemption from the extended duties.

6.3 Country of origin

Origin for trade remedy measures is determined by non-preferential rules of origin. In general, goods may be considered to have originated in a named country if either:

  • they were wholly produced within that country
  • two or more countries are involved in the manufacture of a product — where the last substantial operation in the processing or the manufacture of the goods has resulted in the manufacture of a new product, or has represented an important stage of manufacture and is economically justified

6.4 How to check a product has been made subject to any anti-dumping or countervailing duty measures

Read the trade remedies notices.

Use the online Trade Tariff: look up commodity codes, duty and VAT rates.

You will need to:

  1. Enter the commodity code for your product.

  2. Change the ‘trade between UK and’ to the country of origin of the product

  3. Select the ‘import’ tab

  4. Scroll down to ‘trade remedies, safeguards and retaliatory duties’.

  5. Check if your exporting producer is listed and if an additional code and specific conditions apply to their product.

  6. Producers that are not listed will get either the highest anti-dumping or countervailing duty rate, or both.

You may need to enter a 4-digit additional code to your customs declaration, to get a lower rate of anti-dumping or countervailing duty. You may also need to hold, and if asked, produce a specifically worded and signed statement on the invoice from the producer or supplier. You will need to declare this using the appropriate document code on your declaration.

If the same rate applies to all imports from a particular country, there will be no need for an additional code.

6.5 Accuracy of the online Tariff

It’s not always possible to the Department for International Trade to update the online Tariff on time for when an anti-dumping measure takes effect. Before you clear your goods through customs, we recommend that you check:

  • DIT’s website for new anti-dumping measures
  • Trade remedies notices
  • Trade Tariff: look up commodity codes, duty and VAT rates

6.6 The effect of an undertaking

If an investigation for the products you import is in progress, it may be stopped before a provisional or final duty is imposed. For this to happen, your supplier will need to give a satisfactory ‘undertaking’ to stop any damage to UK producers caused by the dumping or subsidy.

If your supplier has given an acceptable undertaking, they must also:

  • show that they’re fulfilling all the conditions of their undertaking on a continuous basis
  • allow verification of the information they have provided to demonstrate this

If this does not happen, the Trade Remedies Authority may end the undertaking arrangement and declare the invoices concerned to be invalid, anti-dumping or countervailing duty will then be incurred on those imports.

7. General provisions

7.1 How to apply for a refund of anti-dumping or countervailing duty

To claim a repayment for all or part of the anti-dumping duty paid, you must be able to show either:

  • the products imported were not dumped
  • the margin of dumping was less than what the anti-dumping measures were based on

More information is set out in the Trade Remedies Authority guidance.

7.2 Time limits for claiming a repayment

You must submit your claim within 6 months of the date the goods were entered for home use.

7.3 Evidence needed to support a claim

Read the Trade Remedies Authority guidance to find out what information and evidence or documents (or both) you need to include in your application.

7.4 Goods under inward processing arrangements

If you hold an inward processing authorisation, you can suspend anti-dumping and countervailing duty until your goods enter free circulation.

To qualify for duty suspension under inward processing the goods must be:

  • entered to inward processing for the purposes of an authorised process
  • put to an eligible disposal

7.5 Goods under outward processing arrangements

If you are authorised to reimport goods under outward processing arrangements, any anti-dumping or countervailing duties (or both) due is chargeable on the compensating products.

7.6 Warehousing

If you placed imported goods in a customs warehouse, you’ll pay the anti-dumping or countervailing duty rate in force when those goods are removed from the warehouse and are entered to free circulation. This is irrespective of the date the goods entered the warehouse.

8. What to do if you disagree with a customs decision

8.1 Right to be Heard (before a decision)

Before we send customs decision you may disagree with, we’ll tell you why we intend to do so. This explanation is made in line with your Right to be Heard.

The explanation letter is your Right to be Heard and will give you a period of 30 calendar days for you to provide more information to us before we make a decision.

8.2 Reviews and appeals (after a decision is issued)

If you disagree with a customs decision you can either:

  • ask for a review by writing back to the decision maker within 30 days of the date of the letter, giving the reasons why you do not agree with their decision
  • appeal directly to the Tribunal who are independent of HMRC

If you choose to have your case reviewed, you’ll still be able to appeal to the Tribunal if you disagree with the outcome.

More information about reviews and appeals is in leaflet HMRC1: HM Revenue and Customs decisions — what to do if you disagree.
