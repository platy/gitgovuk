This methodology statement provides information on how the ‘COVID-19: mental health and wellbeing surveillance report’ (the report) is produced. The information will help users to:

  • understand the strengths and limitations of the report

  • understand the methods used to create the report

  • decide suitable uses for the report

Introduction

The report provides a summary of currently available evidence, focusing on adult and children and young people’s (CYP) mental health and wellbeing in England or the UK, during the Coronavirus pandemic (COVID-19). The aim of the report is to:

  • locate intelligence (near to real time evidence derived from eligible studies, surveys, support services etc.) according to specific inclusion criteria

  • summarise and provide a regularly updated report based on the intelligence related to mental health and wellbeing, and the impact of COVID-19

  • monitor and report on the changes in self-reported mental health symptoms, wellbeing levels and experiences during the COVID-19 pandemic

  • identify and report on the determining factors associated with levels or changes in symptoms during COVID-19 based on population groups in England

Users and uses

The findings from the report may be of use to policy makers, service planners, service providers, researchers, academics, and mental health and wellbeing charities.

This kind of surveillance of impacts on mental health is logistically challenging, but is expected to provide value beyond conventional evidence reviews in situations where:

  • the ability to respond to a changing environment is needed and decisions may need to be made quickly

  • the certainty in existing evidence is limited, posing a barrier to decision-making

  • there is likely to be new research evidence emerging that would inform decisions

Strengths

The strengths of the report are that:

  • it includes regular and frequent updates, which provide a timely flow of intelligence for users during the COVID-19 pandemic, based on identification of near to real-time evidence

  • the content of the tracker report will improve over time, in line with the improvements in the evidence base

  • the collation of a set of studies which include validated survey questionnaires (for example PHQ-9 symptoms of depression, GAD-7 symptoms of anxiety, GHQ-12 symptoms of mental distress) and self-reported experiences and impacts of COVID-19 on mental health and wellbeing

  • the inclusion of data prior to the COVID-19 lockdown will enable understanding of changes (where data is available)

  • information about vulnerable groups is included (where data is available)

Caveats

There are a number of important points and limitations which the user should consider when reading the report and these are that:

  • some trends reported are based on repeated cross section data, that is, the survey is carried out with a new sample of participants at each successive time point, therefore, any variation seen may not reflect changes for the same group of participants

  • in order to provide as close to real time surveillance as possible, studies included in the tracker report may be working papers or awaiting publication, and as such may not have been peer reviewed

  • there is a delay in publishing more robust analytical studies due to the time it takes to collect, clean and analyse the data and then make available to the public

  • the report is not exhaustive, it is the result of a pragmatic approach to the gathering, and provision, of useful evidence during the COVID-19 pandemic

  • the report is not a systematic review – see evidence assessment approach below

  • judgement about significance or accuracy of findings is not part of the review process as a light touch appraisal is carried out

  • the mention of specific studies does not imply that they are endorsed or recommended by PHE in preference to others of a similar nature that are not mentioned

  • the evidence provided within the report should not be viewed as final, definitive or authoritative

  • findings taken from the various data sources have not been verified by PHE

  • the report contains information which should be classed as ‘experimental statistics’

  • changes in incidence and prevalence of severe mental illness (SMI) during the pandemic are not covered in detail within the intelligence materials that this document reports on

Structure and content of the tracker report

The report is divided into distinct sections, these include:

Important findings (chapter 2)

Summary of important findings from selection of studies based on 2 themes: overall change in population mental health and wellbeing, and group specific changes in mental health and wellbeing.

Measures of anxiety, depression, loneliness and life satisfaction (chapter 3)

To assess consistency of findings, this section triangulates weekly surveys and a number of studies, against 4 key themes.

Further weekly data (chapter 4)

This chapter adds to and complements chapter 3. It provides further measures of population level mental health and wellbeing on:

  • major stress - related to COVID-19 and finances

  • thoughts of death/self-harm

  • self-reported self-harm

  • reported feeling happy, sad and/or scared

Summaries of academic research (chapter 5)

Updates of the latest published findings from a set of studies on mental health and wellbeing during COVID-19, based on plain English summaries of the study abstracts.

Telephone and online support service use (chapter 6)

Reporting as up to date as possible data and commentary from telephone and online support service providers including logins, new registrations, contacts and issues raised. Usage statistics include Citizens Advice clients, Anxiety UK contacts, Mind Infoline contacts, XenZone ‘Qwell’ logins, Togetherall, Rethink.

Children and young people (chapter 7)

This chapter is presented in three parts

Important findings

A summary of published evidence on CYP mental health and wellbeing in England and the UK during the COVID-19 pandemic, includes evidence on:

  • COVID-19 direct and indirect impact on children and young people’s mental health

  • reported symptoms of mental ill-health

  • changes in reported symptoms over the pandemic

  • how reported symptoms may vary by characteristics

  • experiences, risk and protective factors associated with mental health outcomes during COVID-19

Weekly tracking data

Compares parent self-reported experiences on 3 key themes:

  • anxiety

  • major stress relating to COVID-19

  • major stress relating to finance

Telephone and online support service use

Reporting as up to date as possible data and commentary from telephone and online support service providers including logins, new registrations, contacts by phone and issues raised. Usage statistics from XenZone ‘Kooth’ and Young Minds.

Method for data collection and analysis

Adults

Geography

Covers whole of UK or England (publications in English)

Eligibility

16 years to adult for all groups.

Group 1: Pre-existing ongoing longitudinal surveys with mental health and/or wellbeing variables, data from pre and post March 2020 – which capture changes between pre and during COVID-19 pandemic (good to moderate quality). Survey uses validated diagnostic tool(s).

Group 2: Longitudinal surveys established in March, April and May 2020 with mental health and/or wellbeing variables, with at least one follow-up point. Captures changes through the COVID-19 pandemic (good to moderate quality). Survey use validated diagnostic tool(s).

Group 3: Additional longitudinal and/or cross-sectional surveys that concentrate on specific populations/topic areas and mental health and/or wellbeing variables. Captures data post March 2020 (good, moderate, low quality).

Group 4: Weekly public opinion survey, nationally representative sample with mental health and/or wellbeing variables, which captures data from March 2020, and is ongoing.

Group 5: National mental health and wellbeing remote service provider with weekly service use data available for publication.

Search strategy

One main search conducted at beginning of report production.

Group 1: eligible cohorts selected from Catalogue of Mental Health Measures

Group 2: eligible cohorts selected from COVID-Minds

Group 3: eligible cohorts identified through snowball method (for example academic and professional network), ongoing search throughout reporting period

Group 4: eligible surveys identified through snowball method (for example academic and professional network)

Group 5: snowball method – contacted Orcha (Organisation for the Review of Care and Health Apps) accredited services and other services recommended by experts

Extraction

Reviewers carry out a routine check for updates (new analyses/results/submitted data).

Group 1: Via contact with principal investigators or checking latest results online

Group 2: Via contact with principal investigators or checking latest results online

Group 3: As and when new cohorts are identified via principal investigators

Group 4: Via contact with principal investigators/statistical lead, or checking latest results online

Group 5: Via contact with service provider or data sent to team each week (PHE Reviewer 3). Each reviewer enters data into a standard extraction template

Synthesis

Results are synthesised regularly from the extraction template. Findings from specific groups are allocated to appropriate sections of the report:

  • Important findings – Groups 1, 2 and 3

  • Weekly data and survey triangulation - Groups 1, 2, 3 and 4

  • Weekly data tracking of the general population - Group 4

  • Spotlight - Groups 1, 2 and 3

  • Updates from surveys - Groups 1, 2 and 3

  • Weekly data tracking use of remote support services - Group 5

Children and Young People

Geographical

Covers whole of UK or England (Publications in English)

Group 1:

  • CYP <18 years including evidence pertaining to this age group, for example 16 to 24s. Parents/carers of CYP <18 years

  • published from March 2020 to present, with any data collection from December 2019 to present

  • mental health and wellbeing outcomes (for example, excluding educational outcomes unless linked to wellbeing).

  • published documents including academic literature (including pre-print and working papers), grey literature, government and public sector analysis, public, academic and third sector press releases

  • all qualitative and quantitative methods including opportunity sampled and randomly sampled surveys, qualitative research, administrative statistics, secondary analyses, reviews and secondary reviews

Group 2:

  • CYP 4 to 19 years and parents of CYP

  • weekly public opinion survey

  • nationally representative sample with mental health and/or wellbeing variables

  • captures data from March 2020 and is ongoing

Group 3:

  • CYP 4 to 19 years

  • national mental health and wellbeing remote services provider with weekly service use data available for publication

Search Strategy

Group 1: This is an extensive and complex search strategy along with key words – these are available on request

Group 2: One main search conducted at beginning of report production. Eligible surveys identified through snowball method (for example academic and professional network)

Group 3: One main search conducted at beginning of report production. Snowball method – contacted Orcha (Organisation for the Review of Care and Health Apps) accredited services and other services recommended by experts

Extraction

Group 1: Each reviewer enters data into a standard extraction template. Research team reviews publications retrieved through search, summarises findings related directly to research questions.

  1. Reviewers select studies/sources, decides on relevance for inclusion in tracker. If study not selected by any reviewer, lead reviewer assigns to a reviewer, notifies them via email

  2. Reviewers read study/source, provides summary of key findings in thematic extraction document, including key limitations and notes of interpretation

  3. 20% or 3 studies (whichever is higher number) peer reviewed by another member of review team. Areas for improvement and rationale for final decisions are logged on DH Exchange

Group 2: Via contact with principal investigators/statistical lead, or checking latest results online

Group 3: Via contact with service provider or data sent to team each week

Synthesis

Group 1: Extracted evidence reviewed for relevance to CYP mental health and wellbeing in COVID-19, including experiences relating to COVID-19. Include if:

  1. Supports statements already evidenced in the synthesis unless large number of evidence sources corroborate statement and new source is lower quality and/or earlier fieldwork

  2. Contradicts statements already evidenced in synthesis unless large number of evidence sources already cited contradict same statement and new source is lower quality and/or earlier fieldwork

  3. Supports new statement and new evidence is high standard, for example:

  • representative sample OR

  • new evidence covers specific group of CYP which more rigorous sources have not engaged OR

  • evidence is at least moderate standard, for example large non-representative sample and authoritative source and new evidence is about a new topic which more rigorous samples have not engaged OR

  • at least one other piece of evidence supports new statement

Group 2: Results are synthesised regularly from the extraction template. Findings from specific groups are allocated to appropriate sections of the report.

Group 3: Results are synthesised regularly from the extraction template. Findings from specific groups are allocated to appropriate sections of the report.

  1. Children and Young People Summary - Group 1
  2. Children and Young people – Weekly tracking data - Group 2
  3. Children and Young people - Weekly data tracking use of remote support services - Group 3
