This page explains the mechanisms to manage the budget and expenditure on the Green Gas Support Scheme (GGSS), and the associated annual and quarterly publications. It provides information on scheme budget management for both applicants and investors.

The GGSS aims to encourage the use of anaerobic digestion biomethane plants to increase the injection of green gas into the gas grid, while providing value for money for billpayers. Green gas contributes to carbon emissions savings by reducing the emissions intensity of the UK’s natural gas supply, as well as reducing emissions from waste.

The key mechanisms explained below are:

  • Budget Caps
  • the Annual Tariff Review
  • Degression

There are 4 publications that BEIS is required to publish regularly, and which are also explained below:

  • Expenditure Forecast Statements
  • the Annual Tariff Review
  • Tariff Change Notices
  • Green Gas Levy: rates and exemptions

BEIS is also required to publish the scheme’s budget caps, and its estimates of inflation and quarterly production factors for new plants, all of which are available on this page.

Budget Caps

Overview

To provide robust budget management for the scheme there are 2 separate budget caps which fulfil distinct functions:

  • an annual Application Budget cap (AB)
  • an annual Overall Scheme Expenditure Budget cap (OSEB)

The OSEB is used to set the Green Gas Levy (GGL), which funds the GGSS, and will directly correlate to collection of funds through the Levy. It is designed to provide an overall budget cap against which actual levels of scheme expenditure will be tracked and monitored.

The AB limits estimated spend by acting as a cap against which all applications to the scheme will be checked. Applications will only be approved if there is unallocated AB available to make payments based on their estimated production. Applications for additional capacity will also be checked against the AB.

A differential is set between the AB and the OSEB to build in headroom to minimise the risk of a shortfall between funds collected through the Levy and payments made to producers. There is likely to be variation between the budget allocated to plants on application and actual spend. As the GGL rate is set annually, collection will be fixed for the financial year after the rate has been announced.

The AB cap is hit if there is insufficient budget available to fund the expected eligible production of an application in the current or any future year of the scheme. If this occurs, it will be placed into a queue. Applications will then be processed in the order they applied should additional budget become available either through other applications withdrawing or being rejected. Later applications may be processed should there be sufficient budget available for them, for example if their expected eligible production is lower than for applications higher up in the queue.

The AB and OSEB can also be increased or decreased for current and future years upon review by the Secretary of State for Business, Energy and Industrial Strategy. Any change will take effect from one of 1 January, 1 April, 1 July or 1 October. Queued applications may then progress if additional budget is available.

Budgets will be announced annually by 31 December along with the announcement of the Levy rate for the following year.

Methodology

The AB will be set based on estimated eligible production and therefore estimated spend, using our forecast capacity, deployment, and production factors.

The OSEB sits above this to provide headroom should actual spend be higher than estimated. The differential between the 2 caps provides a risk threshold.

Forecasting for deployment

Budget caps aim to strike the balance between avoiding unnecessary collection through the GGL and ensuring that as far as possible projects can access the GGSS without being placed in a queue. They are set using deployment forecasts based on market intelligence to estimate the total scale of applications to the scheme in a given scheme year.

This process carries inherent uncertainty, particularly for scheme years 2021/2022 and 2022/2023 due to the significant time horizon between the opening of the GGSS and the end of the period that the first 2 levy rates cover (31 March 2023).

The GGSS’s budget caps for the 2021/2022 and 2022/2023 financial years were set before scheme launch on 30 November 2022 at a level that enables deployment levels that are consistent with the central/high deployment scenarios set out in the GGSS final stage Impact Assessment across the remainder of 2021/2022 and 2022/2023 scheme years.

Inflation

Inflation rates are used to uprate the tariff payments made through the scheme. Ofgem will uprate tariff rates using the December CPI inflation rate (published by ONS in January). Differential between AB and OSEB

Following BEIS modelling, a differential of 15% between the AB and OSEB has been determined as proportionate to balance the need to minimise underspend whilst ensuring sufficient funds are collected and deployment is not curtailed. This will remain under review and may be refined as improved data becomes available.

The modelling used to determine this differential has considered a range of factors that contribute to overall uncertainty regarding scheme spend. These include commissioning dates, production factors, potential variation between capacity at TG application and registration, and potential interaction with the Renewable Transport Fuels Obligation.

Spend is allocated to a producer at the point that a Tariff Guarantee is issued based on estimated annual payments. This is calculated based on the maximum injection capacity of the installation as per the Network Entry Agreement, the relevant tariff, a plant’s stated commissioning date, the estimate for inflation in that financial year in table 3 and the production factors in table 2. Applications for additional capacity are calculated using the same methodology, though only the additional capacity will be considered as opposed to the total maximum injection capacity of the installation.

As spend allocated during this process is estimated, there is likely to be a variation between these estimated annual payments and the actual payments made to a plant.

Degression

Objective

The government needs to ensure that the GGSS offers value for money to the billpayer, sufficiently incentivises biomethane production and provides certainty for industry investment.

Degression supports this balance by automatically reducing the tariffs available for new applicants if set expenditure thresholds are met. If forecasts show that we could be paying out more on the scheme than we expected to due to higher capacity deployment, this is a sign that tariffs could be too generous.

The Green Gas Support Scheme Regulations 2021 require BEIS to publish an Expenditure Forecast Statement every quarter, in which a forecast of expenditure over the year ahead is compared to the scheme Expenditure Threshold. If the Threshold is met and a degression occurs, BEIS must also publish a Tariff Change Notice detailing the reduction.

The Green Gas Support Scheme Regulations 2021 also set out the powers for the BEIS Secretary of State to amend tariffs and degression thresholds through the Annual Tariff Review.

Effect

When a degression occurs, this reduces by 10% the tariffs available to subsequent GGSS applicants.

The GGSS degression process will act as follows:

Is the quarterly Expenditure Threshold for expenditure over the year ahead exceeded?

  • if yes - 10% degression
  • if no - no degression

All applications to the GGSS require a Tariff Guarantee. Therefore, once a participant has a tariff awarded and is fully registered on the scheme, they will receive this tariff throughout the GGSS payment period, regardless of any future degressions.

Process

Each quarter that the scheme is open to new applicants, until 30 November 2025, the degression process goes through 4 stages set out below:

1. BEIS forecasts GGSS expenditure over the following 12 months based on data as of 31 January, 30 April, 31 July and 31 October each year. This will forecast expenditure for the year ahead from those dates and will be based on data from GGSS applications made to Ofgem.

2. BEIS publishes these quarterly forecasts in an Expenditure Forecast Statement by 1 March, 1 June, 1 September, and 1 December respectively. If the expenditure forecast exceeds the degression threshold, a degression will be triggered, and these will be accompanied by a Tariff Change Notice, advising that tariffs will be reduced by 10%.

3. Ofgem will publish new tariff rates by the 15th day of the month following the publication of the Expenditure Forecast Statement.

4. Any new tariff will come into effect for subsequent registrations on the first day of the following month.

The Expenditure Forecast Statement used for degression assessments is only based on existing post-Stage 2 applications at the time of assessment. Budget cap forecasts are based on actual expected spend, drawing on market intelligence as well as applications and using production factors rather than the total value of eligible capacity.

Expenditure Forecast Statements and Tariff Change Notices

The GGSS Expenditure Forecast Statements, as stated above, will be published by 1 March, 1 June, 1 September and 1 December each year that the scheme is open to new applicants, until 30 November 2025.

The Statement will compare the year-ahead expenditure forecast to the Expenditure Threshold. If the forecast meets or exceeds the threshold level for the quarter, then a degression will occur.

Tariff Change Notices announce new tariff levels if a degression has been triggered and will be published simultaneously with the relevant Expenditure Forecast Statement.

See Green Gas Support Scheme (GGSS): expenditure forecast statements and tariff change notices for previous Statements and Notices.

Expenditure Threshold Figures

In accordance with the GGSS regulations, BEIS must publish expenditure threshold figures for each quarter. The threshold figures are reviewed as part of the Annual Tariff Review and new thresholds will then be published by 1 September, taking effect from 1 October.

To set the Expenditure Threshold figures BEIS takes deployment forecasts and sets the threshold level on the same basis as the expenditure forecast used in the Expenditure Forecast Assessment, based on the expected total eligible capacity of plants. The thresholds are set based on an application and deployment forecast which is above the central, expected level (see Table 1), as they are intended to represent an unexpected surge.

Opening Tariffs

Tariffs for biomethane injection at GGSS launch are defined in regulations as:

  • Tier 1: Up to 60,000 MWh – 5.51 p/kWh
  • Tier 2: the next 40,000 MWh – 3.53 p/kWh
  • Tier 3: Above 100,000 MWh – 1.56 p/kWh

These tariff levels are informed by the costs and revenues of a typical reference biomethane plant. This understanding was modelled on a combination of desk-based research, market intelligence, ongoing engagement with industry and NNFCC (National Non-Food Crops Centre) research. The detailed evidence on costs underpinning the tariff setting methodology was set out in the final stage Impact Assessment.

Both the degression and Annual Tariff Review mechanisms will only affect the initial tier 1, 2 and 3 tariffs for subsequent applicants. The mechanisms will not alter the tariffs for applicants with properly made Stage 2 Tariff Guarantee applications.

Annual Tariff Review

The Annual Tariff Review (ATR) process works in conjunction with the degression mechanism. Whereas degression is an automatic and rules-based process, the ATR is more in-depth. This reflects their complementary functions, with degressions being designed to respond quickly within year to surges in deployment, while the ATR has the broader function of ensuring production continues to be incentivised at the best value for money.

Objective

The Annual Tariff Review (ATR) aims to ensure tariff payments are sufficient to incentivise deployment on the GGSS, whilst ensuring value for money for the billpayer. Tariff rates that are too high risk overcompensating producers and overburdening the billpayer. Rates that are too low risk decreasing deployment or lowering quality of equipment and feedstocks used, and therefore lowering contributions to carbon budgets.

The ATR will hold, increase or decrease the scheme tariffs for new applicants, with reference to our existing tariff setting methodology. The process will review scheme data, market intelligence, policy costs, degression expenditure forecasts and industry responses to a BEIS call for evidence. The powers to review tariffs were set out in the GGSS regulations.

The GGSS Expenditure Thresholds used for degression will also be reviewed in the ATR and may also subsequently be updated. They will be published as part of the review and updated on this page by 1 September. Any new threshold will come into effect from 1 October, and will therefore be active in the degression assessment for the quarter ending 31 October (published by 1 December; see the Expenditure Forecast Statement section).

Assessment

Information assessed during the ATR is expected to include:

  1. Detailed plant construction costs from applicants
  2. Cost impact from changes to other government policies
  3. Deployment incentivised through GGSS to date, indicating extent of tariff alignment
  4. A call for evidence
  5. Additional market intelligence inputs; including plant costs and revenues, finance costs and required rates of return.

For the ATR Call for Evidence, BEIS will seek to use a standardised survey, which could cover the above information and additional factors. We expect calls to adapt in future years to best reflect market conditions and the data available to Ofgem.

Process

The ATR process will take place between May and September in 2022, 2023 and 2024. A call for evidence will be published by early June and will provide a minimum of 3 weeks for responses. Quarterly and monthly data provided by Ofgem will also be available to BEIS for the review.

An Expenditure Forecast Statement will also be published on 1 September, at the same time as the ATR. However, while the outcome of the assessment will be published, it will not be binding, as the ATR decision takes precedence for setting tariff levels. The degression mechanism then acts as normal from the quarter following the ATR decision, acting on whatever tariff level is set out in the ATR.

During the ATR assessment, analysts will re-run the tariff setting process as conducted for the final scheme impact assessment. Following consideration of this analysis, the ATR announcement will be made on 1 September and come into effect a month later on 1 October.
