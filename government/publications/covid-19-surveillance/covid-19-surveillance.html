The Surveillance and Immunity studies are part of the UK Health Security Agency (UKHSA) that is responsible for managing several major studies to understand coronavirus (COVID-19). We collaborate with a number of delivery partners, including the Office for National Statistics (ONS), research, academic and commercial partners.

We have now begun a new phase of living with the SARS-CoV-2, as outlined in the COVID-19 Response: Living with COVID-19 plan that was published on 21 February 2022. The Government will continue monitoring the virus through maintaining scaled back surveillance studies and other data sources including genomic sequencing. The studies help inform current and future Government policy decisions.

Surveillance studies

COVID-19 Infection Survey

April 2020 – ongoing

The COVID-19 Infection Survey (CIS) started in April 2020 and is being delivered with the University of Oxford, IQVIA and other partners.

This study uses repeat household visits to establish the proportion of the general population who have SARS-CoV-2. Participants are tested whether they report having symptoms of COVID-19 or not. Blood samples are also taken from a proportion of participants to test for SARS-CoV-2 antibodies.

This study was originally set up to gather data from England, but has been expanded to include Wales, Northern Ireland and Scotland. The study tests 150,000 participants every fortnight.

Vivaldi 1

May 2020 to June 2020

This study was launched in May 2020 and focuses on understanding what proportion of care home healthcare workers and residents in England tested positive for SARS-CoV-2, as well as the virus’ transmission routes and mitigating factors within care homes. Residents and carers received swab testing through the national care home testing programme, whilst care home managers took part in a national telephone survey.

Vivaldi 2

June 2020 – ongoing

This study was launched in June 2020 and builds on the findings of Vivaldi 1. The cohort study has expanded its initial set of 100 care homes in the Four Seasons Health Care chain to cover approximately 340 homes in England and 15,000 participants.

Vivaldi 2 uses trained phlebotomists to draw blood from care-home residents and staff, which is then used to test for both antibody and cellular immune responses to COVID-19. This data is cross-referenced with DHSC-delivered swab testing in care homes to provide a detailed picture of prevalence, seroprevalence, transmission and immunity over time.

The study also provides data on the efficacy of the vaccine as the mass-vaccination programme is rolled out in care homes nationwide.

In addition, samples of venous blood from care-home residents participating in Vivaldi 2 are undergoing immunological analyses to explore cellular immunity to SARS-CoV-2. This research, conducted by the University of Birmingham, is an integral part of the Vivaldi project and is sometimes known as ‘Vivaldi 3’.

ONS COVID-19 Schools Infection Survey

October 2020 – ongoing

Commissioned by DHSC, the ONS collects data and conducts analysis, alongside UKHSA (formerly Public Health England (PHE)) and the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine (LSHTM), to understand and assess SARS-CoV-2 infection, as well as its transmission within schools. Phase 1 of the study (SIS1), which concluded in July 2021, involved repeated antibody and PCR testing of students and staff, drawn from up to 150 schools (both primary and secondary) in England. Phase 2 (SIS2, from autumn 2021) focuses on antibody testing, whilst acquiring insights into pupils’ health and wellbeing as well as preventative measures implemented in schools.

Immunity studies

Sars-CoV-2 Immunity and REinfection EvaluatioN (SIREN)

June 2020 – ongoing

This is a major study testing blood samples and swabs obtained from healthcare workers who work in a clinical setting to see if infection with SARS-CoV-2 protects them from future episodes of infection. The study also assesses vaccine effectiveness and the effect of antibody protection against variants.

It was set up in April 2020 to assess the risk of re-infection in healthcare workers who have tested positive for antibodies.

Protective Immunity from T-Cells in Healthcare workers (PITCH) – UKHSA

March 2020 – ongoing

This study is a partnership between multiple universities to understand the T-cell responses in healthcare workers. It focuses on the immunity that might come from having been previously infected with SARS-CoV-2 and what this means. Following the rollout of the COVID-19 vaccines, the study is also analysing how vaccination alters people’s T cell response to fight the virus.
