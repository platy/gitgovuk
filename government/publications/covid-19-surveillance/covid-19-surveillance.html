Robust population surveillance programmes are essential to understanding the rate of SARS-CoV-2 infection, and how the virus is spreading across the country. They help us to assess the impact of measures taken to contain the virus, to inform current and future government actions.

The Surveillance and Immunity Directorate of the UK Health Security Agency’s (UKHSA) Joint Biosecurity Centre (JBC) is taking forward Pillar 4 of the UK testing strategy. Under this strategy, the directorate’s remit is to commission and manage a number of major studies to understand to what extent COVID-19 (the disease caused by the virus) is present and affecting the wider population.

This also includes looking at how the immune system has responded to SARS-CoV-2 and how this varies over time. We use the data and insights generated from these studies, including analysis of the impact of the vaccine rollout, to help strengthen our scientific understanding of COVID-19, inform the government’s policy decisions and work across the testing programme.

We collaborate with a number of delivery partners, including the Office for National Statistics (ONS), research, academic and commercial partners.

Surveillance studies

COVID-19 Infection Survey – Office for National Statistics

April 2020 – ongoing

The COVID-19 Infection Survey (CIS) was started in April 2020 and is being delivered with the University of Oxford, IQVIA and other partners.

This study uses repeat household visits to establish the proportion of the general population who have SARS-CoV-2. Participants are tested whether they report having symptoms of COVID-19 or not. Blood samples are also taken from a proportion of participants to test for SARS-CoV-2 antibodies.

This study was originally set up to gather data from England, but has been expanded to include Wales, Northern Ireland and Scotland. The study tests 150,000 participants every fortnight.

REACT-1

April 2020 – ongoing

The REACT-1 study was launched in April 2020. It is a large population study that measures the prevalence of SARS-CoV-2 in the general population and estimates how quickly the virus transmits between people (R value) across a 2-week period each month.

Up to 125,000 unique participants take part each month and the results provide insights about SARS-CoV-2 and COVID-19, in addition to the prevalence of emerging variants.

REACT-2

May 2020 – ongoing

The REACT-2 study was launched in May 2020 to estimate how many people have already had SARS-CoV-2 in England. Over 150,000 participants took an antibody finger prick test at home to provide useful insight about changes in antibody levels in the general population.

The study has also helped scientists learn more about the usability and accuracy of different antibody tests, as well as how vaccines affect antibody levels in people.

ZOE COVID Study

This is a not-for-profit initiative started by health science company ZOE in collaboration with King’s College London in March 2020. It supports vital COVID-19 research, is one of the world’s largest ongoing studies of COVID-19 symptoms and facilitates hotspot detection. The ZOE app provides unique insights into asymptomatic and symptomatic information across the UK with up to one million users logging on a weekly basis, and is supported by a grant from UKHSA to support its ongoing data collection.

Vivaldi 1

May 2020 to June 2020

This study launched in May 2020 and focuses on understanding what proportion of care home healthcare workers and residents in England tested positive for SARS-CoV-2, as well as the virus’ transmission routes and mitigating factors within care homes. Residents and carers received swab testing through the national care home testing programme, whilst care home managers took part in a national telephone survey.

Vivaldi 2

June 2020 – ongoing

This study was launched in June 2020 and builds on the findings of Vivaldi 1. The cohort study has expanded its initial set of 100 care homes in the Four Seasons Healthcare chain (FHSC) to cover approximately 340 homes in England and 15,000 participants.

Vivaldi 2 uses trained phlebotomists to draw blood from care-home residents and staff, which is then used to test for both antibody and cellular immune responses to COVID-19. This data is cross-referenced with DHSC-delivered swab testing in care homes to provide a detailed picture of prevalence, seroprevalence, transmission and immunity over time.

The study also provides data on the efficacy of the vaccine as the mass-vaccination programme is rolled out in care homes nationwide.

In addition, samples of venous blood from care-home residents participating in Vivaldi 2 are undergoing immunological analyses to explore cellular immunity to SARS-CoV-2. This research, conducted by the University of Birmingham, is an integral part of the Vivaldi project and is sometimes known as ‘Vivaldi 3’.

Schools Infection Survey

October 2020 – ongoing

Commissioned by DHSC, the ONS collects data and conducts analysis, alongside UKHSA (formerly Public Health England (PHE)) and the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine (LSHTM), to understand and assess SARS-CoV-2 infection, as well as its transmission within schools. Phase 1 of the study (SIS1), which concluded in July 2021, involved repeated antibody and PCR testing of students and staff, drawn from up to 150 schools (both primary and secondary) in England. Phase 2 (SIS2, from autumn 21) focuses on antibody testing, whilst acquiring insights into pupils’ health and wellbeing as well as preventative measures implemented in schools.

CoMix study: LSHTM UK social contact study

March 2020 – ongoing

CoMix is a unique social contact survey study which was launched in March 2020 by the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine (LSHTM), with data collected by Ipsos MORI.

The study has been funded by several bodies, including the National Institute for Health Research (NIHR), the Medical Research Council (MRC), and the UK Health Security Agency (UKHSA) since October 2021.

Participants report the total number and nature of direct contacts they had the day before they take the survey: people they had at least a brief face-to-face conversation with or with whom they had any sort of skin-to-skin contact.

Participants are also asked additional questions, such as about whether they wore a facemask on the day of reported contacts.

The data is analysed to produce a fortnightly report for SPI-M-O and SAGE which tracks the reproduction number, participants’ mean contacts over time and explore differences in regions.

sKIDs surveillance study – PHE (now known as UKHSA)

June 2020 to September 2020

At the beginning of June, PHE, now UKHSA, launched this pilot in partnership with the Department for Education (DfE) through funding from DHSC.

The study focused on providing a better understanding of prevalence in pre and primary schools in the last half of the summer term of 2020 after national lockdown measures began to ease. It was designed to provide the government with insights to inform the response to SARS-CoV-2 and the management of education settings in a safe and secure way.

This small pilot focused on blood and swab tests of pre-school, primary and staff from 130 schools across England to better understand rates of transmission of COVID-19.

UK Biobank SARS-CoV-2 serology study

May 2020 – December 2020

This study was established in May 2020 by UK Biobank, in collaboration with the University of Oxford. It was designed to establish the proportion of Biobank participants who had previously had the infection, the strength of different groups’ antibody responses and how this varied over time.

It used a fixed cohort of 20,000 registered with UK Biobank and their families who self-administered finger prick blood samples once a month for 6 months.

Immunity studies

Sars-CoV-2 Immunity and REinfection EvaluatioN (SIREN) – UKHSA

June 2020 – ongoing

This is a major study testing blood samples and swabs obtained from healthcare workers who work in a clinical setting to see if infection with SARS-CoV-2 protects them from future episodes of infection. The study also assesses vaccine effectiveness and the effect of antibody protection against variants.

It was set up by PHE, now UKHSA, in April 2020 in response to the need to assess the risk of re-infection in healthcare workers who have tested positive for antibodies.

Protective Immunity from T-Cells in Healthcare workers (PITCH) – UKHSA

March 2020 – ongoing

This study is a partnership between multiple universities to understand the T-cell responses in healthcare workers. It focuses on the immunity that might come from having been previously infected with SARS-CoV-2 and what this means. Following the rollout of the COVID-19 vaccines, the study is also analysing how vaccination alters people’s T cell response to fight the virus.
