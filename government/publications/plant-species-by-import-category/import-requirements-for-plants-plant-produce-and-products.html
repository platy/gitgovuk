Introduction

This guide applies to plants and plant products which need phytosanitary control to be imported from a third country to Great Britain (England, Scotland and Wales).

It will not fully apply to imports from the EU to Great Britain until the end of the phased import process in July 2022. Read the guide on how to import plants and plant products from the EU to Great Britain and Northern Ireland for more detail on the phased approach.

If you need help with import notifications, you can call the Animal and Plant Health Agency (APHA) helpline on 03300 416 999 or email APHAServiceDesk@apha.gov.uk.

It is divided into 5 categories of phytosanitary control and 6 commodity groups, explaining which category and commodity groups different products fall into.

This guide is based on ‘The Plant Health (Phytosanitary Conditions) (Amendment) (EU Exit) Regulations 2020’ and references other legislation where appropriate, for example in the cases of genera that have species exemptions or when a derogation applies to certain countries only.

Plants, fruit, vegetables, cut flowers, soil and other regulated objects (for example, machinery) fall into 5 categories of phytosanitary control:

  • ‘Prohibited’ - Can only bring into Great Britain if a scientific research license or derogation is obtained.
  • ‘Prohibited (pending risk assessment)’ - Can only bring into Great Britain if risk assessed by Defra. Excludes in vitro material, naturally or artificially dwarfed woody plants for planting of those genera.
  • ‘Regulated and notifiable’ - Can only bring into Great Britain if accompanied by a phytosanitary certificate and advanced notification of landing.
  • ‘Regulated’ - Can only bring into Great Britain if accompanied by a phytosanitary certificate. After applicants complete a customs entry, a small number of these consignments may need advanced notification on CHIEF (Customs Handling of Import and Export Freight).
  • ‘Unregulated’ - Can bring into Great Britain without a phytosanitary certificate or advanced notification.

The guide is divided into the following commodity groups:

  • plants for planting
  • fruit
  • seeds for planting
  • cut flowers, parts of plants and vegetables
  • tubers (including potatoes)
  • other

Plants for planting

All plants for planting are regulated. They’re either regulated and require pre-notification or are prohibited.

Plants for planting include, but are not limited to:

  • plants that can only grow in water or soil that’s permanently saturated with water
  • bulbs
  • tubers
  • rhizomes
  • algae

A full definition of plants for planting can be found in Article 2 of the Plant Health Regulation 2016/2031.

Machinery and vehicles which have been operated for agricultural or forestry purposes All third countries Regulated and notifiable

Growing medium includes:

  • bark
  • biowaste (food waste, garden waste, kitchen waste, sewage sludge, manure, and sawdust)
  • humates
  • humus
  • potting composts (consisting in whole or in part of soil, or solid organic substances such as parts of plants, humus, including non ‘pure’ peat or bark)
  • soil conditioner
  • soil improvers

This is provided they are free of organic material such as roots, grasses, leaf litter and other parts of plants.

Growing medium does not include:

  • chalk
  • coir
  • diatomaceous earth
  • gravel
  • gypsum
  • humic acid (must be pure and unused)
  • inorganic soil additives
  • iron ore
  • leonardite
  • lignite
  • perlite
  • pure sand
  • pure clay
  • pure peat
  • rock
  • salt
  • synthetic and inert soil conditioners
  • vermiculite
  • volcanic pumice
