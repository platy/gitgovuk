1. General points on testing

Public Health England (PHE) does not accredit nor does it advise on individual COVID-19 point of care testing devices or platforms, but has contributed to and led Department of Health and Social Care (DHSC) commissioned evaluations of specific devices and published relevant findings.

There are a variety of terms in common use to describe COVID-19 tests. They are not interchangeable, and care should be taken with terminology. A point of care (POC) or near-person (NP) test provides a result near to the person being tested, instead of sending their specimen to a laboratory. A POC/NP test may be used within a healthcare setting such as a hospital, a general practice or a pharmacy, or in other settings such as a residential care home, a school, or in a person’s home. Generally, results from POC/NP testing are much more rapid (around 30 minutes) than traditional laboratory testing.

The results of COVID-19 tests that detect different parts of the virus or immune response provide different clinical information that often overlaps:

  • nucleic acid tests (for example, polymerase chain reaction (PCR) tests) indicate whether an individual has or recently had infectious virus. The majority of those with positive PCR results are infectious but the virus can persist in some individuals for many weeks and therefore a repeat test is not recommended in someone whose symptoms have resolved
  • coronavirus virus protein antigen tests (for example, lateral flow devices (LFD) for antigens (Ag)) indicate whether a person has the virus now and is likely to be infectious to others
  • tests for coronavirus antibodies (proteins produced in response to the infection) are conducted on convalescent blood sent to a laboratory or by dropping a fingerprick blood specimen into an LFD test for antibodies. They indicate if someone has had the virus before - see guidance on coronavirus (COVID-19) antibody testing

There are a range of technologies that are used to provide POC/NP testing. POC/NP tests which detect viral genomic material through amplification are similar in sensitivity to laboratory-based reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) tests, whereas POC/NP tests which detect viral protein, such as LFD Ag tests are less sensitive compared to PCR for detecting any virus. However, LFD Ag tests are simpler to perform and detect similar proportions of those individuals with high amounts of virus.

The Medicines & Healthcare products Regulatory Agency has published a guide to COVID-19 tests and testing kits.

2. Types of testing available and recent evaluations

The standard RT-PCR test for current infection is performed on machines by expert laboratory staff in a central laboratory. Other nucleic acid based tests use portable devices to provide rapid POC testing outside a laboratory, such as the Simple Amplification Based Assay (SAMBA II), the DNA Nudge platform, and various loop-mediated isothermal amplification (LAMP) platforms and assays.

An LFD antigen (Ag) test uses a colour change on a test strip, without amplification, to directly detect non-nucleic acid antigens of coronavirus. To independently and robustly evaluate the suitability of COVID-19 LFD Ag POC/NP tests for comprehensive and repeated testing of whole populations, there is an agreed established process. By November 2020, four LFD Ag tests were shown to have a sensitivity of over 70% of all RT-PCR positives and, critically, these tests detected almost all (over 95%) with high viral loads (the current infections that are most infectious when tested). Sensitivity was not affected by the presence or absence of symptoms in those tested, although test performance was closely related to the level of training of those conducting the test. Five LFD Ag tests have been shown to detect the new variant of SARS-CoV-2 that became widespread in South-East England and London in December 2020.

Results of field evaluations of LFD Ag tests for testing asymptomatic persons in England are available. Although test sensitivity in a subset of samples in the large-scale SMART city-wide testing programme in Liverpool was 40% when compared to RT-PCR, LFD Ag testing identified two-thirds of persons with higher RT-PCR SARS-CoV-2 viral loads, and around a quarter of the almost 3,800 infectious people were detected solely through LFD Ag testing. The report highlighted that the sensitivity of LFD Ag tests should be considered alongside the faster time taken to get a test result. The evaluation of a pilot LFD Ag testing service for visitors to 20 care homes in low coronavirus prevalence areas as a risk reduction measure highlighted service improvements that should be implemented.

A recent field study of university students in the United States who were symptomatic and RT-PCR positive reported LFD Ag test sensitivity to be 80%. In students who were asymptomatic and RT-PCR positive, LFD Ag sensitivity was 41%, despite the prevalence overall of SARS-CoV-2 infection being 5%. LFD Ag testing detected almost 90% of the RT-PCR positives with a high viral load as indicated by having a cycle threshold of 30 or under.

LFD antibody tests have also been developed. These indicate whether a person has had infection with COVID-19 at some point in the past and cannot diagnose current infection. A phased evaluation process for LFD antibody POC/NP tests is being established, similar to that for antigen tests.

Both LFD antigen and antibody POC/NP tests should be distinguished from the postal self-sampling kits that are being sent to homes. These self-sampling kits are used by someone to take their specimen and return it to a central laboratory for testing.

3. Relevant regulations and guidance

A COVID-19 POC/NP test must have a valid CE mark to ensure its design and manufacture achieves the performance specified for its purpose and is safe to use. Following registration with the UK Medicines & Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA) or other competent body, the manufacturer places a CE mark on the product thereby declaring that the test meets the required legal criteria. Obtaining a CE mark includes preparation of ‘Instructions for Use’ that specify whether a test should be performed by a trained professional or may be done by a lay person. MHRA has provided specific guidance on how to self-declare as a private COVID-19 testing provider.

MHRA Guidance on the principles to be followed for the management and use of POC/NP test devices, emphasises that the local hospital pathology laboratory should play a key role in developing and supporting a POC/NP testing service, and strongly recommends that POC/NP testing service providers participate and perform adequately in an External Quality Assessment (EQA) scheme and that a suitable and sufficient safety risk assessment be conducted prior to use. The UK National EQA Service for microbiology will have an EQA scheme for LFD Ag POC/NP tests in place by end January 2021.

The Advisory Committee on Dangerous Pathogens (ACDP) has provisionally classified SARS-CoV-2 as a Hazard Group 3 (HG3) pathogen. Interim guidance has been issued for the safe handling and processing of specimens from patients with possible or laboratory confirmed COVID-19 (see Section 3 about risk assessment and POC/NP testing).

From December, the 2020 Health Protection (Coronavirus, Testing Requirements and Standards) (England) Regulations require all private coronavirus test providers to be engaged with the United Kingdom Accreditation Service (UKAS), and to have achieved UKAS accreditation for coronavirus testing, no later than June 2021. Also, coronavirus testing is now exempted as a regulated activity under the Health and Social Care Act 2008 (Regulated Activities) Regulations 2014.

The Health Protection Regulations have been amended to require the reporting of positive and negative SARS-CoV-2 and influenza test results by POC/NP service providers. PHE has issued reporting guidance and built a secure POC/NP test reporting web portal to capture these data. A template with a specific format is available from PHE’s POC/NP testing support team (poct.contact@phe.gov.uk) and organisations providing SARS-CoV-2 and influenza POC/NP testing services should register to notify results.

4. Managing COVID-19 RT-PCR and LFD (antigen or antibody) POC/NP test results

The following is general guidance for managing the results of coronavirus RT-PCR, LFD antigen and antibody POC/NP tests used on persons with current or recent symptoms as part of a clinical service.

A range of factors may affect what an LFD Ag test result means including: the level of training of the person conducting the test, the presence or not of possible COVID-19 symptoms, whether the person tested has had prior laboratory confirmed SARS-CoV-2 infection, whether the person is a contact of someone with recent laboratory confirmed SARS-CoV-2 infection, whether the person has contacts in a setting that is experiencing a COVID-19 outbreak, and whether the person has had COVID-19 vaccine.

5. RT-PCR and LFD Antigen test results in those with COVID-19 symptoms

Anyone with COVID-19 symptoms should begin self-isolation immediately (as should other members of their household) and should request a laboratory RT-PCR test through NHS Test and Trace. They can take a test by either attending a testing centre or by registering and receiving a home sampling kit.

  • if the RT-PCR test result is positive, this means they are infected and likely to be infectious. They must complete 10 days isolation; the isolation period includes the day the symptoms started and the next 10 full days (or 14-days if hospitalised). Other household members should complete their 10 day contact isolation period
  • coronavirus RNA is usually detected by RT-PCR from two days prior to illness onset to 14 days after. In some individuals RT-PCR testing will detect remnants of viral RNA for much longer and so repeat RT-PCR or other testing is not recommended within 90 days
  • if the RT-PCR test is negative, there is no evidence of virus RNA at the time of testing and the person is highly unlikely to be infected or infectious. False negative RT-PCR test results can occur early or late in the illness. If the person is unwell and admitted to hospital with a compatible syndrome and abnormal X-ray findings, further sampling should occur before removing from isolation. A negative test can occur with poor sampling or very early or late in the illness

In the event that someone with COVID-19 symptoms has an LFD Ag POC/NP test:

  • if the LFD Ag test result is positive, this means that viral antigens are present, so they are infected and highly likely to be infectious. The likelihood of the test giving a false positive result is low. False positives are an important consideration if testing asymptomatic individuals when the prevalence of disease in the community is low
  • in some cases, including for self-reported positive LFD Ag test results, they may be asked to take an RT-PCR test. In these situations, they, and other members of their household, should self-isolate immediately they become aware of the positive LFD test result. The legal duty to self-isolate and the tracing of non-household contacts are triggered by a positive result from the RT-PCR test
  • if the LFD Ag test is negative, this means that no viral antigens have been detected, and they are highly unlikely to have very high levels of virus at the time the test was done. However, they may have low levels of coronavirus and they, and other members of their household, should continue to self-isolate until a RT-PCR test has been undertaken. Further management will depend on the investigatory RT-PCR test result. A negative RT-PCR test is essential for them to be released from self-isolation

Occasionally an individual with COVID-19 like symptoms will begin self-isolation, recover within one to two days and have a negative LFD Ag POC/NP test on the third day or later. Conducting a prompt investigatory RT-PCR test is the best way to inform both the likelihood that their recent symptoms were COVID-19 related and how their contacts should be managed.

Invalid POC/NP test results should be repeated with a new test. If a person with COVID-19 symptoms has two invalid LFD Ag POC/NP tests, they should have an RT-PCR test as soon as possible.

NHS COVID-19 RT-PCR tests are free for those with symptoms, those asked to have a test by a local council or by their hospital, or when a test is considered necessary by a clinical or public health professional.

6. LFD Antigen test results in those without symptoms

MHRA has issued an exceptional use authorisation to the DHSC to allow use of LFD Ag POC/NP testing of asymptomatic persons as part of the COVID-19 National Testing Programme. As a risk reduction/mitigation measure, LFD Ag tests are mainly being used in this programme to test large numbers of people without symptoms. By making testing more available and having rapid results, additional asymptomatic infectious people can be detected and isolated immediately. Large groups including staff, residents and patients in health and social care settings, as well as whole city populations, are being tested by different services including the NHS, local authorities, employers and third-parties.

For those involved in implementing community asymptomatic testing, detailed LFD Ag testing guidance and toolkits, including standard operating procedures and instructional videos for staff, are available separately for the NHS, local authority Directors of Public Health and other organisations such as schools and colleges. The toolkits include materials to carry out large-scale testing in various use cases, including schools and colleges, universities and care homes.

LFD Ag POC/NP testing of persons without symptoms is currently not recommended unless as a formal part of one of the programmes or pilot studies within the National Testing Programme. When managing the results of LFD antigen tests used on asymptomatic persons for public health purposes (for example, repeated testing of asymptomatic persons in the community), additional factors apply such as the prevalence of infection in the group tested, and the frequency of testing of members of the group.

If an LFD Ag test result in an asymptomatic person is positive, the person who has been tested may be asked to take an RT-PCR test, for instance if disease prevalence is low or where the LFD Ag test result is self-reported. In these situations, they, and other members of their household, should begin self-isolation immediately they become aware of the positive LFD test result. The legal duty to self-isolate and the tracing of non-household contacts will be triggered by a positive result from the follow-up RT-PCR test. Where an RT-PCR test is not undertaken, the legal duty to self-isolate and tracing of non-household contacts will begin immediately.

If an asymptomatic individual’s LFD Ag test result is negative, then the timing of the person’s exposure to coronavirus risk is critical to providing further advice. Given the incubation period for COVID-19 is up to 14 days, a negative test result in someone recently exposed simply means the infection is not manifest yet. If tested as a contact, the person should remain alert for possible COVID-19 symptoms until their contact follow-up period has ended. Anyone who receives a negative LFD Ag test result should continue to observe the [‘Hands, Face, Space’](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/coronavirus-covid-19-meeting-with-others-safely-social-distancing/coronavirus-covid-19-meeting-with-others-safely-social-distancing) measures for preventing coronavirus transmission and adhere to any additional guidance that applies to their local area. This includes getting tested if they develop possible COVID-19 symptoms.

7. LFD antibody test results in those who may have had COVID-19 infection previously

Many LFD antibody POC/NP tests are now available and there is great variation in their performance. So far, when compared with high-throughput laboratory ELISA antibody tests, which have a high level of accuracy, independent evaluations of the most promising POC/NP tests have shown each to be less sensitive (study 1, study 2).

The use of LFD antibody POC/NP tests to assist in the diagnosis and management of individual patients is not recommended at present, unless as a formal part of one of the programmes or pilot studies within the National Testing Programme. Should an LFD antibody POC/NP test result, whether positive or negative, be influencing the clinical management of an individual patient’s symptoms, it is essential that a more reliable laboratory ELISA antibody test is performed. Individuals with persistent disease that may be attributable to an earlier COVID-19 infection should be referred to medical care for further investigation.

Though evidence is increasing that the presence of antibodies is protective in the short-term, it does not necessarily mean that someone is immune from catching COVID-19 infection again, particularly over longer time periods. LFD antibody tests to detect vaccine induced antibody has not been evaluated and the tests should not be used for this purpose. Individuals who test positive by LFD antibody test should be reminded to continue to adhere to all social distancing and public health prevention advice.
