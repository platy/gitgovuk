Introduction

18.01. This chapter covers the steps that we will take to ensure that payments and associated fees made to you are justified and that the definitions and conditions for a payment have been met in full.

Overview of Payment Validation

18.02. The validation approach to payments will be determined by type:

  • Delivery Fee
  • Employed job outcome
  • Self-employed job outcome

18.03. The monthly delivery fee payments in year 1 of the contract are made to you based on achievement of your predicted FTE staff in post levels and achievement of agreed Customer Service Standards (CSSs). For the remainder of the contract, full payment of monthly delivery fees will continue to be dependent on achievement of CSSs.

18.04. Each monthly delivery fee payment will be subjected to pre and/or post-payment validation to verify payments to be made/which have been made are accurate and justified.

18.05. The employed outcome payment process is fully automated. DWP validate achievement of the outcome criteria using HMRC Pay as You Earn (PAYE) online earnings data. The determination of outcomes will be solely based on this data and any outcome payments due will be made to you automatically.

18.06. We will carry out manual pre-payment validation checks on all self-employed outcome claims submitted. If volumes make undertaking validation checks on every claim unviable, we may move to either a mix of a pre-payment and post-payment sampling, or a wholly post-payment and extrapolation approach. We may also vary how the checks are carried out.

18.07. All payments made in respect of the Restart Scheme will be made via Provider Referral and Payments (PRaP) directly into your agreed UK bank account. Further information regarding PRaP can be found in Provider Referrals and Payments (PRaP) System Guidance for DWP.

18.08. The very last outcome payment will be made to you no later than 667 days after the last start on the Restart Scheme.

Payment Validation Process

Delivery Fee Assurance

18.09. As referenced in Restart Scheme Provider Guidance Chapter 17 – Funding Model and Claim Process, full payment of your monthly Delivery Fees is subject to you both meeting your forecasted Full-Time Equivalent (FTE) staffing levels and achieving Customer Service Standards (CSSs). See Delivery Fee Payments in the Contract Specification for Delivery Fee deferral and forfeit implications.

Customer Service Standards Measures

18.10. Throughout the life of the contract Performance Managers and Performance Compliance Officers (PCOs) will monitor your performance against the CSSs. If your performance falls below the agreed standards your monthly Delivery Fee payment may be deferred or forfeited. Restart Scheme Provider Guidance Chapter 14 – Performance Management contains more information about these measures and associated financial penalties.

Delivery Fee Standards Measures

18.11. In Year 1 of the contract, full payment of monthly Delivery Fees is also dependant on you meeting the Delivery Fee Standards measure by achieving the monthly FTE staffing levels you detailed in your contract.

18.12. You will be required to submit HR/Payroll information at regular intervals using the Monthly Performance Return (MPR) as set out in Schedule 8.8 of your contract and described in the Restart Scheme Provider Guidance Chapter 14 – Performance Management.

8.13. If during either pre or post-payment checking of Delivery Fee Standards your FTE levels are found to be below 75% of the level detailed in your contract this could be treated as a termination event.

Pre-Payment Checks

18.14. We will carry out pre-payment checks on the information you provide, to confirm the total FTE you and your supply chain employ, in management and direct delivery roles, on the Restart Scheme contract meets the forecasted levels detailed in your contract.

18.15. If your reported FTE levels are the same as or with 95% of those detailed in your contract the maximum payment (applicable to that month) will be released, subject to CSSs also being met.

18.16. If your total FTE employed on the Restart Scheme contract is below 95%, of your forecast a proportionate amount of the monthly delivery fee will be forfeited. For example, if your reported FTE is 93% of the level detailed in the contract 2% of your delivery fee for that month will be forfeited.

Post-Payment Checks

18.17. In addition, we will carry out post payment checks on a sample-basis or via a full census to assure ourselves that individuals included in your FTE return are employed by you and your supply chain partner on the Restart Scheme contract.

18.18. A random sample of at least 50 employees proportionate for each CPA will be drawn. The sample size will be determined by ensuring the output is a statistically significant result given the likely size of the populations from which they will be drawn. In some instances, the sample size may exceed 50 employees.

18.19. Through the post-payment check evidence will be gathered to assure the following:

  • Confirm the correct employee​​
  • Confirm the employee is employed by the correct Provider or one of their Sub-Contractors/ Delivery Partners​​
  • Confirm the employee was employed on Working Day (WD) 1 of the month being checked​​
  • Confirm the employee FTE stated on the MPR is correct and compare it against the bid/ FTE waiver levels

18.20. There will be two stages in the assurance process. All cases in the sample will be subject to a check through Get Citizens Income Information (GCII), this is an independent check utilising real time payment information from HMRC. Where the evidence required, cannot be gathered via GCII, a screen share call will be organised with Providers. This will be a further opportunity for you to present the evidence required for assurance.

Post-Payment Checks – Provider Actions

18.21. An additional MPR template will be sent to Providers via Jaggaer. This will contain the information previously submitted for the month being assured, as well as two additional fields to be completed for each employee:

  • The employee’s National Insurance Number
  • The employee’s Full Time Equivalent (FTE)

18.22. For security reasons the additional template will be password protected, Providers will be informed of the password, separately by email. On receipt of the MPR template via Jaggaer, Providers will have 5 working days to complete the required fields before re-uploading and returning to DWP via Jaggaer.

18.23. Prior to returning the additional MPR template please ensure that that the correct spelling/ version of all employee names is accurate. If you are aware that an employee’s name appearing on the MPR template may not be the same as the employee’s name that appears on their official GCII record, please inform us of this or make a note on the MPR return.

18.24. On completing the additional MPR template please inform us, or make a note on the MPR return, if you expect the employer name appearing on the GCII record, not be the same as the Prime Provider or Sub-contractor name that appears on the MPR template. This may be the case if your payroll name is different to your Prime Provider/ Sub-contractor name.

18.25. No further input will be required by Providers at this stage. A sample will be drawn and the GCII checks will be undertaken by DWP Assurance Officers.

Post-Payment Checks – Provider Actions

18.26. Where the evidence required for assurance cannot be gathered via GCII, the Assurance Team will contact Prime Providers to organise screen share calls. These calls will take place over MS Teams and will be a further opportunity to provide the evidence required for assurance.

18.27. These calls will be pre-arranged and while the Assurance Team will endeavour to organise them at a time that is suitable for you. Where possible they will seek to arrange and conclude these within 10 working days of the initial contact date.

18.28. To assist these calls in running effectively you should ensure that you have suitable personnel in place to take the call and who can navigate the Payroll/ HR screens to show the required evidence.

18.29. Please note that you will not be informed prior to the calls taking place, who the employees are where further evidence is required. This is to ensure that a robust assurance process takes place.

18.30. In exceptional circumstances, such as an I.T. failure an additional screen share call can be arranged to take place within three working days of the original call, if required.

18.31. No results will be shared with you during the screen share calls.

Post-Payment Checks – Results

18.32. Following completion of the post-payment assurance process the results of the checks will be reviewed and verified by DWP’s Delivery Fee Payment Board. Following final sign off, you will receive confirmation of the results. Please note that all Delivery Fee Payment Board decisions are final.

18.33. Following the DWP’s Delivery Fee Payment Board decision, all employee data will be anonymised/ deleted.

Note: Any information shared with DWP by the Provider will be used solely for the purpose of validating Delivery Fee Standards and for no other purpose. Providers may wish to ensure that staff understand that you, as the Data Controller, may have to share your employees’ data with DWP.

Employed Job Outcome Validation

18.34. The employed outcome validation and payment process is fully automated. Participants moving into employed work and meeting the qualifying conditions for an employed outcome payment will be identified by DWP using HMRC PAYE online earnings data.

18.35. Participant earnings will be monitored and tracked for a maximum period of 547 calendar days from the Participant’s start date on the Restart Scheme. During this time reports will be available on PRaP iSupplier portal for you to be able to view:

  • when a Participant’s earnings are first declared to HMRC, following a confirmed start on the Restart Scheme (multiple notifications possible with two or more jobs)
  • when a Participant has achieved income milestones of £1,000 and £2,000 during their employment
  • when DWP is notified that the Participant’s employment has ended (multiple end notifications possible with two or more jobs)
  • when an automatic outcome payment has been triggered once the earnings threshold is reached (final notification)

18.36. You will not have a right to appeal or request reconsideration on decisions made relating to employed outcomes. Only earnings received within the 547-day qualifying period will count towards an outcome

18.37. Once achievement of an outcome has been identified and paid, or 547 days have elapsed from the Participant’s start date on the Restart Scheme, tracking of earnings will cease and the payment line will be automatically closed. See Restart Scheme Provider Guidance, Chapter 13 A/B Restart Scheme Completers and Early Exits for more information about completers.

18.38. To ensure system effectiveness, we may undertake tests of the automated payment system to confirm it is delivering the requirements. Should underpayments or overpayments be identified through these checks, or other avenues, remedial action will be undertaken where appropriate.

Self-Employed Job Outcome Validation

18.39. When you submit a self-employed outcome claim on the PRaP system, the payment will automatically be put on hold pending manual validation. An automated check within PRaP will also confirm that the claim submission date is within 90 days of Date 2 on PRaP. Any claims that are outside this period will be flagged up to PPVT via a ‘Claim Limit’ hold and may fail validation.

18.40. The DWP Provider Payments Validation team (PPVT) will have 30 calendar days from the date your claim was submitted to conclude validation activity and determine whether your outcome claim can be paid.

18.41. To validate each claim submitted, PPVT will use information that is independent of yourself and your supply chain to verify the Participant has been in business on a self-employed basis for a cumulative period which covers 6 individual UC assessment periods within their 547-calendar day qualifying period. See Restart Scheme Provider Guidance Chapter 17 – Funding Model and Claim Process for qualifying period description).

18.42. During manual validation PPVT will in the first instance review information relating to work and earnings held on the UC system to confirm:

  • within the outcome claim period (between Date 1 and Date 2 input to PRaP), the Participant had attended an interview with their Jobcentre Plus work coach to discuss their self-employment status and the Jobcentre Plus work coach has deemed the Participant’s self-employment status as being gainfully self-employed for UC purposes, and
  • self-employed earnings have been declared in 6 separate UC assessment periods, and
  • at least one of the assessment periods when earnings have been declared is within the period of the outcome claim (between Date 1 and Date 2 input to PRaP), and
  • all 6 instances of self-declared self-employment are within the 547-day qualifying period

NOTE: Should the Participant’s earnings exceed their UC entitlement resulting in their claim being closed the validation team will contact the Participant directly to confirm the dates and duration of self-employment.

18.43. If all four of these conditions are met the outcome claim will pass validation and be released for payment.

18.44. Where the UC system checks described above do not result in the claim passing validation, PPVT will go on to examine wider evidence, which could include, but is not limited to:

  • UC Verify Self-Employment ‘to do’ responses
  • UC Claimant Commitment Statements regarding self-employment
  • UC Journal Notes regarding self-employment
  • UC Self-reported earnings to confirm duration
  • Confirmation direct with the Participant themselves
  • Consideration of the Participants UC Conditionality Regime throughout the claim period (Light Touch / Working Enough etc.)
  • Any additional supporting evidence from the UC system or direct from the Participant that can confirm their employment status

18.45. See Annex 1 for further supporting information to help you determine self-employment. This list is not definitive.

18.46. PPVT’s ability to contact Participants is reliant on you recording in PRaP accurate and up to date contact information, including details of any special contact arrangements required. If PPVT have trouble contacting a Participant, they will email you for further information. You will have 5 working days to reply with further details or risk the claim failing validation.

18.47. On completion of manual validation checks there are 3 possible self-employed outcome validation results:

Validation Outcome Outcome Description Subsequent Action
Pass PPVT has confirmed that the outcome definition for self-employment and conditions for payment have been met in full. Payment will be released Outcome counts toward performance.
Fail The outcome definition for self-employment and conditions for payment have not been met.

The claim will be removed from PRaP and will count as one of the three possible attempts to claim a self-employed outcome payment.

All three self-employed claim attempts must, however, be submitted within 637 days of the participant starting on the Restart Scheme.		 When a claim fails validation, you are allowed 2 further attempts to claim.

If your third claim attempt fails it will remain a fail, at this point the line will be closed to prohibit any further self-employed outcome claims.

Tracking of HMRC/RTI data to identify potential employed job outcomes will, however, continue until the Participant either leaves the Restart Scheme early or 547 days have elapsed since their start date on the Restart Scheme.

If an employed outcome is achieved, the claim will be paid and re-categorised as an employed outcome Pass.
Unable to Validate (UTV) PPVT cannot conclusively reach a pass or fail outcome decision within 30 calendar days of the claim being submitted.
Payment will be released, and the outcome will be classed as UTV.

You will be notified that the claim has been classified as a UTV.		 PPVT will continue to undertake checks for a further 60 days. If PPVT definitively confirm the self-employed outcome is a Pass or a Fail, it will be re-categorised.

RTI tracking also continues to day 547, if an employed outcome is achieved, the UTV is re- categorised as an employed outcome Pass.

All UTVs remaining are included in the end of cohort reconciliation and apportionment calculations.

Please Note: UC Assessment Periods Definition: Universal Credit Assessment Periods (AP) run for a complete calendar month and start on the same date every month, starting from the date that the Participant made their claim.

For example, for a new claim made on 6th September, the first assessment period runs from 6th September to 5th October, with a new assessment period starting on 6th October.

The UC AP can vary between January & March if the AP starts on either 30th or 31st of the month (due to only 28/29 days in Feb). E.g. if the AP starts on 30th, the periods may be: 30/1 to 28/2; 1/3 to 29/3.

Self-Employed End of Cohort Unable to Validate (UTV) Apportionment

18.48. Self-employed outcome claims that are still categorised as UTV at the end of the additional 60-day checking period will be added to the cohort population for apportionment and reconciliation calculations.

  • A cohort population is made up of all outcome claims submitted in a single calendar month

  • The apportionment activity is carried out when the 547-day qualifying period for each Participant within the cohort population has been reached (this is the cohort period)

18.49. We will establish a fail rate for each cohort which will be applied to any remaining UTVs once the cohort period has ended. The process and calculations used to establish the fail rate and determine the UTV apportionment value are shown in the example below.

Example:

1) 1000 self-employed outcome claims have been submitted in a month/cohort. After 30-day validation period the results are:

  • 865 passes
  • 105 UTVs
  • 30 fails

This results in an error rate following pre-payment validation of 3 % ((number of fails (30) / cohort population (1000)) * 100 = 3% (rounded))

2) Checks continue on the 105 UTVs for a further 60 days and RTI interest remains open for 547 days in an attempt to capture further Passes or Fails.

3) After the cohort period has ended there are 95 UTV cases remaining. 5 claims have passed validation and 5 have failed, making the new cohort outcome totals:

  • 870 passes
  • 95 UTVs
  • 35 fails

4) The error rate is recalculated resulting a revised rate of 4% (rounded). ((number of fails (35) / (cohort population) 1000)) *100 = 3.5% = 4% (rounded)

5) The final cohort error rate of 4% is applied to remaining 95 cases. Therefore 4% of the value of the 95 UTV claims are apportioned as fails the value of which is recovered from the provider. The value of the recovery may include adjustments in respect of the accelerator model.

18.50. The apportionment results will be shared with you and a financial recovery total will be made as and when required.

End to End Self-Employed Outcome Validation Summary

Period Summary
Day 1 to 30 Claims submitted are subject to validation.

• Passes are released and paid
• Fails are removed from PRaP
• Validation of UTV claims continues (see below)
Day 31 to 90 UTV claims are followed up to see if they can be categorised as a Pass or Fail. The validation process ends at Day 90.
Cohort Period All outcomes submitted in a calendar month are classed as a ‘cohort.’ A cohort ends once the qualifying period (547 days), plus an additional 61 days to allow for data settling This equates to 608 days in total.
End of Cohort Reconciliation Once the 608-day cohort period has ended DWP will carry out system checks on all UTV claims to see if that original payment can be justified. UTV claims that cannot be defined as a pass will be considered for apportionment.
Apportionment Each cohort will have established a pass/fail rate. The fail rate associated with each cohort will be applied to the UTV payments (paid at Day-30) which will be recovered from the provider.

Self-Employment Post-Payment Validation Regime

18.51. We intend to use a pre-payment validation approach for Restart Scheme self-employed outcome claims. If, however, the volume of self-employed outcome claims makes this process unviable, we will review arrangements and may decide to move to either a mix of pre and post payment validation, or a wholly post-payment validation regime. In this event, you would be given prior notification of when the change would happen, and guidance would be updated to reflect the new processes.

18.52. For more general information regarding the approach to post-payment validation see Generic Provider Guidance, Chapter 5.

Additional DWP management checks

18.53. In addition to the validation principles set out above, we may also conduct management checks to further verify the validity and robustness of self-employed outcomes claimed or paid.

18.54. These checks will support the exploration of controls around self-employed outcome payments. Although these management checks will not form part of the standard validation process, they will aid DWP in identifying potential patterns or fraudulent activities.

18.55. Where we identify a substantial number of self-employed claim errors such as high volumes of Participant contact details being incorrect, insufficient information provided leading to high UTV outcomes or self-employed start/end dates are unaligned, DWP may review your internal assurance processes.

Annex 1: Additional Supporting Information

A Participant is considered to be in gainful self-employment where all of the following apply:

  1. the Participant is carrying on a trade, profession or vocation as their main employment, and

  2. their earnings from that trade, profession or vocation are self-employed earnings, and

  3. the trade, profession or vocation is organised, developed, regular and carried out in expectation of profit

If any of these conditions are not satisfied, the Participant is not considered gainfully self-employed.

1. Is the Participant carrying out a trade, profession or vocation where they are not employed by someone else?

The following illustrate what is meant by, carrying out a trade, profession or vocation.

Trade

For example, a self-employed plasterer or market stallholder. A Participant is likely to be trading if they:

  • sell regularly to make a profit
  • make items to sell for profit
  • sell online, at car boot sales or through classified adverts on a regular basis
  • earn commission from selling goods for other people
  • are paid for a service they provide

A Participant is probably not trading if they sell some unwanted items occasionally or they don’t plan to make a profit.

Profession

For example, a self-employed accountant or consultant.

Vocation

For example, a self-employed musician or actor.

2. Deciding if earnings are self-employed earnings

To assess if a Participant’s earnings are self-employed earnings for the purposes of Universal Credit and the outcome definition, there are a number of important indicators that should be considered. The following table lists some of these indicators.

Indicators that a Participant’s earnings are from self-employment Additional information
Pay their own tax and National Insurance. Where tax is deducted at source, this may suggest that the employment is not self-employment, however, special arrangements can apply for building sub-contractors.
Are registered as self-employed with Her Majesty’s Revenue and Customs (HMRC) for self-assessment and Class 2 National Insurance contributions and have a unique tax reference number. A unique tax reference number is issued by HMRC when a person registers for self-assessment and may be an indicator of self-employment.
However, having a unique tax reference number is not sufficient in itself to demonstrate whether a person is self-employed.
Can fix the price and/or duration of their work and have control over their costs.  
Have the power to appoint or dismiss and can employ a substitute, for example to cover holidays or sickness A power to appoint a substitute may suggest self-employment.
Length of work contracts. Short contracts may suggest self-employment. Longer contracts with the same company may suggest employment or possibly sub-contracting.
Provide their own equipment.  
Work from home, a workshop, or an office they own or rent. Working from home is also increasingly common for some employees.
The person who engages the claimant for work has no obligation to provide further work. If there is an ongoing obligation, then this may point towards employment.
Can decide the hours they work. The greater the discretion the claimant has, the more likely it is that the work is self-employment.
Can choose which work they accept and/or work for a range of different employers. The more choice / discretion a claimant has, the more likely it is that the work is self-employment.

No single indicator is likely to be conclusive. Instead, these indicators should be used together to:

  • build a general picture of the claimant’s work
  • give an idea of how the Participant, their employer (in the case of sub-contractors) and HMRC view their employment
  • help reach a decision on whether their earnings should be classed as employed or self-employed

3. Deciding if the self-employed activity is the main employment

As part of the gainful self-employment test, a decision is needed on whether the Participant’s self-employment is their main employment. This is important, especially for Participants who are either:

  • undertaking self-employed activities but for only a few hours or on a low paid basis
  • both self-employed and also working as an employed earner

The following are indicators that the self-employed activity is likely to be a Participant’s main employment if they:

  • spend more than half of their available work hours on self-employment
  • spend more time on, or earns more from, their self-employment than other activities, for example they work more hours in self-employment than as an employed earner
  • are starting a new business and business-related activities take up most of their expected number of hours per week
  • consider their self-employment as their main job goal
  • have significant self-employed earnings, even where self-employed hours are low

This is not a complete list.

4. Deciding if the self-employment is organised, developed, regular and carried out in expectation of profit

The following are possible indicators that the self-employment is organised, developed, regular or carried out in expectation of profit and must be taken in to account when making a decision:

  • whether the activity is undertaken for financial gain, the claimant does not need to have made a profit yet for the activity to be considered in expectation of profit
  • the number of hours spent each week working and the regularity of the work
  • any business plan or steps taken to increase income from the activity
  • how Her Majesty’s Revenue and Customs (HMRC) regard the activity
  • how much work is in the pipeline
  • whether the claimant is actively marketing or advertising for work

This is not a complete list.

Sub-contractors

People who are contractors or sub-contractors may be treated as self-employed for Restart Scheme self-employed outcome purposes.

To help determine whether a contractor or sub-contractor is self-employed or employed consider the Participant’s activities against the self-employment indicators and the information below.

A contractor who has a contract to provide goods or services to a client may sub-contract some of the work to other people known as sub-contractors.

A sub-contractor is:

  • not an employee of the contractor
  • a self-employed person who enters into a contract with another contractor to do a particular job or provide a service as part of a larger project
  • normally paid a fixed fee for the job or services they provide
  • has control over how they do the work
  • often found in the construction industry

In some cases, a building sub-contractor will have a proportion of their earnings deducted at source to pay tax under Her Majesty’s Revenue and Customs (HMRC) Construction Industry Scheme (CIS) arrangements. This will either be 20% or 30%. It will only be 30% in a small proportion of cases where the sub-contractor does not provide a unique tax reference number.

Despite tax being deducted at source, the sub-contractor could still be considered as self-employed and will not be subject to the pay as you earn system. A sub-contractor will not have National Insurance deducted at source.

Consideration must also be given to whether the self-employment activity is the claimant’s main employment in the same way as for other claimants, for example where a sub-contractor:

  • alternates between spells of self-employment with work as an employee
  • tops up their self-employed earnings with employed earnings, depending on the work or terms of work available to them

Companies and directors

Participants trading through a company are treated in the same way as someone who has chosen not to organise their self-employment in this way. Therefore, it does not matter if a claimant chooses to:

  • register their business as a company
  • make themselves a director
  • pay themselves as an employee of the company, through shares and dividends, or by other means

Where the claimant has structured their business as detailed above or have declared they are a company director, to be treated as self-employed you must determine whether they:

  • are in a similar position to a sole owner or partner in a company which is carrying on a trade
  • have few shareholders, significant influence over the company and a significant say on the day to day running of the business
  • are gainfully self-employed or not
