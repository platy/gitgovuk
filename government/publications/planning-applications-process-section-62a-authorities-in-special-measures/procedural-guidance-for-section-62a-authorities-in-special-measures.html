1. Introduction

1.1 Background to Section 62A Applications

1.1.1. From 1 October 2013 the Growth and Infrastructure Act inserted two new provisions into the Town and Country Planning Act (1990) (‘the Act’). In particular, Section 62A allowed major applications for planning permission, consents and orders to be made directly to the Planning Inspectorate (acting on behalf of the Secretary of State) where a local planning authority has been designated for this purpose. Subsequently amendments were made by the Housing and Planning Act 2016 to expand the type of application beyond major development.

1.1.2. The Secretary of State can designate a Local Planning Authority (LPA) where he considers it is not adequately performing its function in determining applications. An LPA can be designated where it has not met the threshold for determining applications within the statutory period (or an extended period which has been agreed with the applicant before the end of the initial statutory period). At the time of publication the thresholds are:

  • 50% or fewer of an authority’s decisions on major development (Major development and non-major development are defined in Regulation 3 of the Town and Country Planning (Section 62A Applications) (Written Representations and Miscellaneous Provisions) Regulations 2013, as amended (also see appendix 2); and
  • 65% or fewer of an authority’s decisions on non-major development (Major development and non-major development are defined in Regulation 3 of the Town and Country Planning (Section 62A Applications) (Written Representations and Miscellaneous Provisions) Regulations 2013, as amended (also see appendix 2).

1.1.3. LPAs are measured separately on their performance in determining applications for major development and non-major development. LPAs can be designated for major development, non-major development, or both. Unitary authorities are measured separately for their district and county matter applications.

1.1.4. Where an LPA has been designated, an applicant may choose to submit an application to either the LPA or the Planning Inspectorate. Only applications for development which falls within the category(s) to which the designation relates can be submitted to the Planning Inspectorate. Certain types of applications, including householder applications, are excluded and cannot be submitted to the Planning Inspectorate (see Appendix 2).

1.1.5. The purpose of this Procedural Guide is to provide additional information to applicants, designated authorities, local communities and other stakeholders involved in the Section 62A process. This guide does not have statutory status. This document should be read alongside the Frequently Asked Questions for local communities.

2. Pre-application process

2.1 Introduction

2.1.1. Under the provisions of Section 62A, applicants can apply directly to the Planning Inspectorate for advice and assistance at the preplanning application stage. This will be similar to the best practice approach adopted by LPAs, with the emphasis on early dialogue to ensure an efficient and effective application process. Preapplication advice is given without prejudice and it does not necessarily follow that the outcome of any formal application will be the same.

2.1.2. Once pre-application advice has been provided, a copy will be sent to the LPA. At this stage the advice will not routinely be published; however pre-application enquiries may be subject to freedom of information requests and it may be necessary to disclose certain details in response to requests for information (Made under the freedom of information act 2000 or environmental information regulations 2004). Once an application is received for a similar proposal by the same applicant the preapplication advice will normally be published as part of the application documentation.

2.2 What will it cost?

2.2.1. Where the Planning Inspectorate gives advice about applying for planning permission, under Section 62A a fee will be payable. This will be calculated on an hourly rate. The hourly rate fees are set by the Secretary of State and are published online. Rates are set to ensure the cost of pre-application advice does not exceed the cost of providing it. Where a site visit is carried out the cost of this, including traveling time, will form part of the hourly rate charged. All expenses incurred will also be added to the charge.

2.2.2. Prior to the start of the pre-application process a charging strategy will be agreed between the applicant and the Planning Inspectorate. Only once a charging schedule is in place will the pre-application process commence.

2.3 What is the role of the Planning Inspectorate?

2.3.1. It is not the role of the Planning Inspectorate to remove a designated LPA’s ability to provide good quality and timely advice to applicants. Applicants can choose whether to seek pre-application advice from the relevant LPA and/or the Planning Inspectorate.

2.3.2. A Planning Officer will be appointed; they will act as the main point of contact and lead the pre-application discussions. For complex major cases, an Inspector may also be appointed to provide strategic advice and assistance on the main issues.

2.3.3. The role of the Planning Officer will primarily be to provide written advice to applicants on development proposals. The applicant should also engage with key stakeholders, such as statutory consultees, and provide copies of any responses to the Planning Inspectorate. It may still be necessary to liaise with the LPA in respect of certain matters, such as the content and structure of any section 106 agreements where applicable.

2.3.4. A site visit will usually be carried out by the planning officer. However in some cases providing pre-application advice based on a desk based assessment may be appropriate. The applicant should indicate they would prefer this when making the submission; however this will result in some limitations to the scope of advice that can be given. The Planning Inspectorate will consider whether it is appropriate to provide the advice on this basis; this will not always be the case, for example it is unlikely to be appropriate for major development proposals or where a heritage asset is likely to be affected.

2.3.5. Further detailed information might be requested before an application is ready to be submitted. There is no minimum or maximum timescale for the pre-application process.

2.4 What is the role of the Relevant Local Planning Authority?

2.4.1. An LPA that has been designated will still play an important role in the pre-application process.

2.4.2. A designated LPA will be expected to provide the following information to an applicant and the Planning Inspectorate as part of the initial pre-application process:

  • copies of relevant local land and policy designations or land allocations;
  • copies of relevant development plan policies, strategies, supplementary planning documents, guidance, and development briefs etc.;
  • information relating to approaches to Community Infrastructure Levy rates and planning obligations;
  • full details of the planning history of the proposed development site;
  • a list of relevant local community groups, including Parish Councils and other organisations and stakeholders; and
  • details of other development proposals that are likely to affect a particular scheme (eg on an adjacent site).

2.5 What is the role of the applicant?

2.5.1. It is not the purpose of this guidance to set out excessively prescriptive requirements for applicants seeking pre-application advice. The Planning Inspectorate, however, will expect applicants to provide the following information:

  • a completed pre-application form (Please refer to Appendix 1 for a copy of the pre-application form);
  • relevant plans drawn to scale showing the location of the site, and confirmation of the relevant LPA;
  • relevant plans or information;
  • a supporting statement identifying what the main issues are considered to be, what consultation has been carried out to date and a summary of stakeholder comments, and how it is intended that the pre-application process will shape the development of the proposal.

2.5.2. Consultation must take place early in the process so that stakeholders may positively influence proposals before submission.

2.5.3. For major development proposals, applicants are strongly encouraged to carry out community consultation separately, alongside discussions with the Planning Inspectorate and the LPA.

2.5.4. Meaningful consultation should also take place with statutory consultees. For example, if the application site is known to be in an area identified to be at risk from flooding then it is strongly advised that initial investigations are prepared and pre-application consultation is undertaken with the Environment Agency and any other relevant consultees. Any advice provided by a consultee should be included in the pre-application documents submitted to the Planning Inspectorate.

2.6 Planning performance agreements

2.6.1. For larger and more complex development proposals the Planning Inspectorate may recommend that applicants sign up to a Planning Performance Agreement (PPA). A PPA at the pre-application stage would act as a project management tool to ensure large and complex projects can progress and evolve efficiently and effectively. A PPA is likely to also include the LPA and set out timescales for responses to requests for information and responses from stakeholders. A template PPA is available on the Planning Inspectorate’s website.

2.6.2. Where appropriate, a PPA may also extend beyond the preapplication stage and set out a timescale for submission and determination. This, however, is more appropriate for very large schemes.

2.7 Overview of the pre-application advice procedure

The applicant will submit a pre-application form and other relevant information, including any advice provided by statutory consultees. A charging schedule will be agreed with the Planning Inspectorate.

The Core Documents will be requested from the LPA by the Planning Inspectorate. The Planning Officer will review the submission and carry out the site visit (where relevant).

Where a Planning Performance Agreement (PPA) has been requested, or is considered necessary, this will be progressed by the Planning Officer. Where appropriate, a PPA used at the pre-application stage will also directly involve the LPA.

For major development proposals, a development project meeting will be held between the applicant and the Planning Officer.

Formal advice will be issued by the Planning Inspectorate to the applicant. This advice may request further information or details on a particular issue, recommend revisions to a scheme, or advise on further stakeholder involvement for the applicant to carry out.

Where necessary additional meetings will be arranged, led by the Planning Officer. This will lead to further formal advice from the Planning Inspectorate.

Subject to progress the application will be ready for submission.

NB - For larger and more complex proposals applicants may continue to provide further information or amend plans for several weeks throughout the pre-application process, however further consultation may need to be carried out before for the application is submitted.

3. Submission of Applications

3.1 What can be submitted to the Planning Inspectorate?

3.1.1. The Planning Inspectorate can only accept applications within the boundaries of a designated LPA. Designations are made separately for major and non-major development (For unitary authorities major applications are considered separately for district level applications and county level applications). There are some types of application that have been excluded and cannot be submitted to the Planning Inspectorate. Definitions of major and non-major development and the excluded development types are provided in Appendix 2.

3.2 Pre-submission checklist

3.2.1. Before submitting an application for a development proposal directly to the Planning Inspectorate under Section 62A, applicants are strongly encouraged to:

  • identify what the main issues are likely to be with reference to the National Planning Policy Framework, the development plan, supplementary guidance documents and issues raised by preapplication community consultation or advice;
  • ensure that all the issues identified are adequately and appropriately addressed in the application submission. This should be included within a Planning Statement or in an accompanying letter submitted with the application;
  • carryout pre-application discussions with any key stakeholders including statutory consultees; and * where appropriate, prepare a draft planning obligation or unilateral undertaking to address issues which will be raised by the development proposal. This should be submitted with a planning obligations statement, identifying how the obligation would meet the tests of the Community Infrastructure Levy Regulations 2010.

Where the proposal is for major development, applicants should also:

  • carry out appropriate pre-application community consultation;
  • carry out pre-application discussions through either the Planning Inspectorate or the LPA; and
  • request an Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) screening opinion from the Planning Inspectorate for proposals which may have significant environmental effects (screening opinions will be issued within 3 weeks and a scoping opinion within 5 weeks, unless more information is required or an extension of time is agreed);

3.3 Validation requirements

3.3.1. A valid application is one that complies with the national validation requirements and meets the relevant requirements of the designated LPA’s published local list.

3.3.2. The national validation requirements are:

  • a completed application form;
  • an ownership certificate, with details of owners and tenants notified, and (in the case of proposed underground mineral workings) a copy of the local press advertisement and site notice;
  • a plan which identifies the land to which the application relates;
  • such other plans, drawings and information necessary to describe the development;
  • a Design and Access Statement (where relevant, see Appendix 3 of this Guide);
  • the required fee; and
  • in the case of applications for the approval of reserved matters, a copy of the outline planning permission which relates to the application.

3.3.3. Any plans and drawings must be drawn to an identified scale and, in the case of plans, must show the direction of North.

3.3.4. Most designated LPAs will have their own local list of validation requirements, which may add to or expand the criteria above. Applicants will be expected to meet the requirements of the Local List. The type of information required under the national validation checklist, can be found in Appendix 4 of this Guide.

3.3.5. In addition, a validation checklist is contained at Appendix 5. All applicants are encouraged to enclose a completed checklist with all application submissions as this will assist the validation process. Some proposals would benefit from additional supporting information, even when this is not a validation requirement.

3.3.6. Where pre-application discussions have been carried out with the Planning Inspectorate, the necessary validation requirements will be discussed prior to submission.

3.3.7. The fee charging schedule at the application stage is exactly the same as that set for LPAs. Application fees are payable to the Planning Inspectorate on the same basis as an LPA and are calculated using the fees regulations (The Town and Country Planning (Fees for Applications, Deemed Applications, Requests and Site Visits) (England) Regulations 2012). There are similar exemptions for a second application relating to development on the same site and applications following withdrawal of an earlier application or refusal of permission (commonly referred to as the “free go”). Such exemptions, however, only apply where an application has previously been submitted to the Planning Inspectorate (with the required fee). Please note that the exemption does not apply where the original application is submitted to the Planning Inspectorate but the subsequent application is submitted to the local planning authority or vice versa. Where a decision has not been made within 26 weeks the planning application fee will be refunded (unless a longer period has been agreed in writing with the applicant).

3.4 How should the application be submitted?

3.4.1. Where possible all applications should be submitted electronically. For applications received in this way, all communications from the Planning Inspectorate will also be carried out electronically. It is therefore important to ensure that email filters do not prevent communications from the Planning Inspectorate.

3.4.2. Where applications are not submitted electronically, three hard copies of all documents and information must be provided.

4. Determination of applications

4.1 The role of the Planning Inspectorate

4.1.1. Applications submitted under Section 62A are dealt with by a dedicated team. Each application is managed by a Case Officer, who is responsible for ensuring that all the necessary processes are followed and the correct information is received.

4.1.2. The Case Officer will work with the LPA and liaise with the applicant and any relevant third parties. A copy of the application documents will be made available on the Planning Inspectorate’s website. Responses will be published on the website after the close of the representation period. The application documents will also be available to view at the relevant LPA offices.

4.1.3. The Planning Inspectorate will appoint a person to determine the application. The appointed person will be provided with a copy of the application documents, representations and any other relevant documents including development plan policies. Consultation with statutory consultees and the designated LPA will be carried out by the Planning Inspectorate. Where required, the Planning Inspectorate will also publish a notice advertising the application in a local newspaper.

4.2 The role of the designated Local Planning Authority

4.2.1. The designated LPA has a statutory duty to complete a questionnaire and provide information about the site, planning policy, and constraints in the area. Where the LPA has provided pre-application advice to the applicant, this can also be submitted; however if it is submitted the LPA must be content that it is published on the Planning Inspectorate’s website. The LPA also must carry out its normal notification duties, which may include erecting a site notice and/or writing to the owners/occupiers of adjoining land. The LPA is expected to undertake non-statutory consultation where necessary.

4.2.2. The LPA is also a statutory consultee and must provide a substantive response to the consultation within 21 days. This should include:

  • the policies and any guidance, that are considered relevant;
  • responses from any internal and non-statutory consultees;
  • set out any amendments to the scheme, or additional information the LPA, considers to be necessary;
  • a recommendation, with reasons, for whether planning permission should be granted or refused;
  • provide detailed comments on any submitted planning obligations, or set out any matters that the LPA considers should be secured through an obligation; and
  • a list of conditions in the event that planning permission is granted. Consultations and representations

4.2.3. Statutory consultees are required to provide a substantive response within 21 days.

4.2.4. Where interested parties wish to make representations about a proposal, these should be made in writing directly to the Case Officer at the Planning Inspectorate, quoting the relevant reference number. The Planning Inspectorate encourages representations to be submitted electronically via email. The name of the Case Officer is also published on the website.

4.2.5. Consultation responses and representations must be received by the deadline given (usually 21 days from the start of consultation). Any representations submitted after this date may not be taken into account.

4.3 Community Infrastructure Levy and planning obligations

4.3.1. The Community Infrastructure Levy (CIL) allows LPAs to raise money for new infrastructure directly from development projects. Planning obligations, also referred to as Section 106 Agreements, mitigate the impact of unacceptable development to make it acceptable in planning terms. Please refer to Appendix 6 of this Guide for further information on CIL and planning obligations.

4.4 Can applications be amended?

4.4.1. Applicants are strongly encouraged to engage in appropriate and meaningful pre-application discussions with the Planning Inspectorate and interested stakeholders before submitting an application. Successful pre-application engagement is very likely to reduce the need to negotiate amendments to a proposal after submission of an application.

4.4.2. Where it appears that modest changes to the scheme, or the submission of additional information, may enable planning permission to be granted, the appointed person may issue preliminary comments, on a without prejudice basis. In these circumstances the applicant will be allowed the opportunity to revise the scheme and/or submit additional information. The applicant, however, will be expected to agree to an extension of time (see below) to the determination period to enable further consultation if a proposed amendment is accepted. In such circumstances further notification will be necessary, inviting comments from interested parties. The application will then be determined on the basis of the additional information/updated scheme, taking account of any further representations.

4.4.3. The extension of time agreement will be proportionate to the time required to consider the amendments, taking account of the time required by the applicant to prepare the additional information, the further notification period, and the associated administration. In cases where an extension of time is not agreed the application will proceed to a decision on the basis of the original submission.

4.4.4. Where the appointed person considers that modest amendments or additional information is unlikely to resolve issues of concern; it will not be appropriate to invite the applicant to amend the scheme. In such cases the appointed person will proceed to a decision. The detailed reasons for the decision will allow the applicant to consider whether a significantly revised proposal is likely to overcome the reasons for refusal. The applicant will have the opportunity to seek pre-application advice on any subsequent revised scheme.

4.5 Extension of time agreements

4.5.1. There may be occasions where the complexity of the issues raised by a development proposal are such that it will be necessary to agree an extension of time for the determination of the application.

4.5.2. Where an agreement for an extension of time is necessary the Case Officer will set out:

  • the reasons for the extension of time;
  • any additional information considered necessary;
  • the date required for the submission of any additional material; and
  • the revised determination date for the application.

4.5.3. On receipt of the agreement, a revised timetable for determining the application will be published on the relevant page of the Planning Inspectorate’s website.

4.6 Called-in planning applications

4.6.1. Under section 77 of the Town and Country Planning Act 1990 the Secretary of State has powers to direct an LPA to refer an application to the Secretary of State for decision. This is what is meant by a ‘called-in’ application.

4.6.2. Although an application may be submitted to the Planning Inspectorate under Section 62A, this process will not change. The Secretary of State may ‘call-in’ an application submitted to the Planning Inspectorate if planning issues of more than local importance are involved.

4.6.3. In such circumstances, the normal procedures for all other applications which are called-in by the Secretary of State will apply.

4.7 Can applications for listed building consent be submitted to the Planning Inspectorate?

4.7.1. Applications for listed building consent can be submitted to the Planning Inspectorate, but only where the works to the listed building are connected with an application for planning permission which has also been submitted to the Planning Inspectorate under Section 62A. The application for listed building consent must be submitted on the same day as the planning application and must be deemed valid within 10 days of the accompanying planning application.

4.8 Overview of application process

4.8.1. The following two charts provide an overview of the expected timescales for the determination of a Section 62A application. It should be noted that no provision is made within the timescales for an amendment to a proposal. A revised or extended programme is likely to be necessary to consider proposed amendments to a scheme.

5. Procedure to be followed

5.1 Criteria to determine the procedure to be followed

5.1.1. Only planning applications relating to development of a significant scale, and which raise issues which cannot be clearly understood from the written submissions will require a hearing. It is expected that the vast majority of applications for non-major development will be determined by written representations.

5.1.2. Shortly after the end of the representation period, the Planning Inspectorate will make a decision as to which procedure is appropriate. The decision will be published on the website. The written representations procedure

5.1.3. Where an application is determined through the written representations procedure, the application will be determined by the appointed person on the basis of the written submissions following a site inspection.

5.2 What is a Section 62A hearing?

5.2.1. Hearings for determining applications under Section 62A are formal, public meetings led by the appointed person. The purpose of the hearing is purely for oral representations to be made, both for and against the development proposal.

5.2.2. The format of the hearing is different to hearings held for planning appeals. Questions may be asked of participants by the appointed person, however, it is not an inquisitorial process and there will be no testing of evidence or cross-examination of expert witnesses. The decision, whether to grant or refuse planning permission, will not be made at the hearing.

5.3 Date and notification of the hearing

5.3.1. The timetable for applications made under Section 62A will enable development proposals to be progressed efficiently, openly and fairly. Although each case will vary, where there is to be a hearing the date will be fixed at the earliest practicable opportunity after the expiration of the representation period. The hearing will normally be held within 5 weeks of the end of the representation period, and sooner if a hearing is held for non-major development.

5.3.2. When a hearing date has been fixed, the Planning Inspectorate will notify all interested parties. Not less than 2 weeks’ notice of the hearing date will be given for applications for major development and not less than 5 working days for hearings in respect of non- major development. Details will also be published on the Planning Inspectorate’s website for Section 62A applications. The requisite notice will include the date, time and place of the hearing along with the name of the appointed person.

5.3.3. To ensure that hearings can take place at an appropriate venue and within the prescribed timeframe the Planning Inspectorate will liaise with the applicant and the designated LPA throughout the course of the application. In most cases it is expected that the designated LPA will provide a suitable venue for the hearing.

5.4 Issues Report

5.4.1. At least 5 working days before the hearing date, an Issues Report will be published online, by the Planning Inspectorate. Its purpose is to inform participants at the hearing of the issues that are considered relevant to the development proposal. The report may also set out some background information in respect of the proposal and the application site and provide the agenda for the hearing. Representation at the hearing

5.4.2. The hearing is a meeting which can be attended by the public, however, the only persons entitled to appear at the hearing and give oral representations are:

  • the applicant;
  • the designated planning authority;
  • any Councillor of the designated planning authority for the ward in which the site, or any part of the site, to which the relevant application relates is situated;
  • any authority (including a Parish Council) consulted in relation to the relevant application; and
  • any person who made representations in relation to the relevant application within the representation period, and, when making those representations, requested to be heard.

5.4.3. Those who are entitled to speak at the hearing may do so in person, or be represented. Prospective participants are reminded that the purpose of the hearing is not to test evidence or crossexamine witnesses. Any persons entitled to make representations should confirm they wish to speak at the hearing to the Planning Inspectorate prior the hearing.

5.5 What will hearings involve?

5.5.1. The appointed person will first summarise the main issues set out in the Issues Report, and invite representations from those who have registered to speak. An agenda for the hearing will be circulated at, or in advance of, the hearing and this will set out a list of speakers registered both for and against the proposal.

5.5.2. Although there is no formal time-limit to the length of representations, the agenda will identify each speaker and set out the order of speakers. Within the agenda the appointed person may set a time limit for comments to be made to ensure those entitled to speak have an opportunity to do so.

5.5.3. Where the appointed person considers that representations are irrelevant or repetitious, they may refuse to permit further comments on a particular subject. The appointed person may also require any person behaving in a disruptive manner to leave the hearing. Once all parties entitled to participate in the hearing have made their oral representations the hearing will be closed.

5.5.4. In the event that a hearing needs to be adjourned, this will be formally set out by the appointed person. The date, time and location of the adjourned hearing can be announced by the appointed person and no further, formal written notification is required.

5.6 Openness and transparency

5.6.1. Hearings are open to journalists and the wider public. Provided that it does not disrupt proceedings, anyone will be allowed to report, record and film the event, including the use of digital and social media. The appointed person will advise people present at the start of the event that the proceedings may be recorded and/or filmed, and that anyone using social media during or after the end of the proceedings should do so responsibly.

5.6.2. If any party wishes to record or film the event using equipment larger than a mobile phone, tablet, compact camera, or similar; or if recording is likely to involve moving around the venue during proceedings, they should contact the Planning Inspectorate in advance to discuss arrangements. This is so that measures can be put in place to minimise disruption.

5.7 Acceptance of late material at, or before, the hearing

5.7.1. Where written representations are submitted outside of the formal representation period, then it will be at the appointed person’s discretion whether or not they are taken into account. It is unlikely that the appointed person will accept additional written material at the hearing.

5.7.2. The Planning Inspectorate therefore strongly encourages all representations, from all interested parties to be made within the representation period set out by the relevant notification.

5.7.3. Representations received after the hearing has closed may be returned and so may not be put before the appointed person for consideration.

5.8 The site visit

5.8.1. A site visit will be carried out by the appointed person. The site visit may occur before or after the hearing (where one is held). Where relevant parts of the application site can be seen from public land, the visit will be conducted on an unaccompanied basis. In other cases, or where access is required to a site, arrangements will be made for the appointed person to have access to the site.

5.8.2. The site inspection is not an opportunity to present representations or evidence. As such it will usually be appropriate for the applicant to arrange suitable access to the site, and the appointed person will not need to be accompanied by other parties. Where an accompanied visit has been scheduled, but an interested party cannot attend, the site visit will go ahead in their absence.

5.8.3. Any interested party who considers the appointed person should view the site from neighbouring land or buildings should make this request in writing in their representation, including a contact number. If the appointed person considers it is necessary to visit neighbouring land, the Case Officer will make the necessary access arrangements.

5.9 Procedure after the representation period and hearing (where held)

5.9.1. Following the representation period, site inspection, and the hearing (if held), the appointed person will prepare a written statement setting out the decision and the reasons for it. This will take into account the development plan policies, national planning policies, the application documents, all representations made, and any other relevant information. In some very limited cases, it may be necessary for the appointed person to take into account new information. If this occurs, there will be an opportunity for further representations to be made.

5.10 Issuing of the decision

5.10.1. The appointed person will issue a formal decision notice incorporating a statement setting out the reasons for the decision. If the application is approved the decision will also list any conditions which are considered necessary.

6. After the decision

6.1 Is there a right of appeal?

6.1.1. There is no provision to appeal the decision, irrespective of the outcome. Similarly, there is no provision to appeal directly against the imposition of any conditions imposed on a permission or consent under Section 62A.

6.1.2. Judicial review is the only way in which a decision made by an appointed person on an application under Section 62A can be challenged. A claim for the judicial review of a decision must be made within 6 weeks of the date of issue. Further information on the judicial review process and contact details for the relevant administrative courts can be found online

6.2 Who makes sure development is carried out in accordance with the planning permission?

6.2.1. Where planning permission has been granted under Section 62A the LPA continues to have the responsibility for monitoring the implementation of the permission, ensuring that it is carried out in accordance with the approved plans and any attached conditions. Applications for the approval of details required by condition must be submitted to the LPA. Applications for variation or removal of conditions can also be made to the LPA.

6.2.2. If the LPA considers that the development does not comply with the details of the planning permission, it has the power to take enforcement action. 8.2 Appendix 2 – Applications covered by S62A and excluded applications

For the purposes of section 62A, ‘major development’ and ‘non-major development’ are defined within Regulation 3(5) of the Town and Country Planning (Section 62A Applications) (Written Representations and Miscellaneous Provisions) Regulations, as amended by The Town and Country Planning (Section 62A Applications (Amendment) Regulations 2016.

Major development is:

  • the winning and working of minerals or the use of land for mineral working deposits:
  • waste development;
  • the provision of dwellinghouses where o the number of dwellinghouses to be provided is 10 or more; or o the development is carried out on a site having an area of 0.5 hectares or more and the number of dwellings is not known;
  • the provision of a building or buildings where the floor space to be created by the development is 1,000 square metres or more; or
  • development carried out on a site having an area of 1 hectare or more.

Non-major development is development that does not meet/exceed any of the thresholds above.

There are a number of types of applications that cannot be submitted to the Planning Inspectorate under section 62A:

  • applications where the development has already been carried out (As described in section 73A of the Town and Country Planning 1990 Act, as amended);
  • householder applications (As described in article 2 of the Town and Country Planning (Development Management Procedure) (England) Order 2015);
  • applications for development without complying with conditions (As described in section 73(1) of the Town and Country Planning 1990 Act, as amended);
  • applications for permission in principle;
  • applications for technical details consent (As described in section 70(2ZZB) of the Town and Country Planning 1990 Act, as amended);
  • applications for approval of details reserved by condition;
  • applications for advertisement consent; and
  • applications for lawful development certificates.

Applications for the approval of reserved matters can be submitted to the Planning Inspectorate provided the outline planning application to which it relates fell within the category of development for which the relevant planning authority has been designated.

An application for listed building consent can only be submitted to the Planning Inspectorate where it is connected to an application for planning permission submitted under section 62A. Both applications must be submitted on the same day and the application for listed building consent must be considered a ‘valid application’ within 10 working days of the related planning application being considered valid.

8.3 Appendix 3 – When a design and access statement is required

A design and access statement includes information about the design principles and concepts that have been applied to the development concerned; and how issues relating to access to the development have been dealt with. It must:

  • explain the design principles and concepts that have been applied to the development;
  • demonstrate the steps taken to appraise the context of the development and how the design of the development takes that context into account;
  • explain the policy adopted regarding access, and how policies relating to access in relevant local development documents have been taken into account;
  • state what, if any, consultation has been undertaken on issues relating to access to the development and what account has been taken of the outcome of any such consultation; and
  • explain how any specific issues which might affect access to the development have been addressed.

A design and access statement is required for:

  • All applications for major development unless it is solely for:

(a) engineering or mining operations; o a material change of use of land or buildings; or

(b) waste development.

  • Non-major development, in a designated area, where it consists of:

(a) the provision of one or more houses; or

(b) the provision of a building(s) where the floor space created is 100 square metres or more.

A designated area for these purposes is a conservation area, or world heritage site.

Waste development is any operational development designed to be wholly or mainly for the purpose of, or material change of use to, treating, storing, processing or disposing of refuse or waste material.

All connected applications for listed building consent will also require a design and access statement. That statement must explain:

  • the design principles and concepts that have been applied to the works;
  • how the design principles and concepts that have been applied to the works take account of—

(a) the special architectural or historic importance of the building;

(b) the particular physical features of the building that justify its designation as a listed building; and

(c) the building’s setting;

Unless the application for listed building consent is only for works that affect the interior of the building, the design and access statement must also:

  • explain the policy adopted as to access, including what alternative means of access have been considered, and how policies relating to access in relevant local development documents have been taken into account;
  • explain how the policy regarding access takes account of:

(a) the special architectural or historic importance of the building;

(b) the particular physical features of the building that justify its designation as a listed building; and

(c) the building’s setting;

  • state what, if any, consultation has been undertaken and what account has been taken of the outcome of any such consultation; and
  • explain how any specific issues which might affect access to the building have been addressed.

Appendix 6 – Community Infrastructure Levy and Planning Obligations

Community Infrastructure Levy (CIL)

The Community Infrastructure Levy (CIL) came into force in April 2010 and allows Local Planning Authorities (LPA) to raise money for new infrastructure directly from development projects. This can then be spent on new roads, schools and hospitals etc.

Rates are set in consultation with the local community and are intended to provide greater certainty by setting out how much money will be required before a project commences. Regulation 123 of the Community Infrastructure Levy Regulations 2010 requires charging authorities to set out a list of projects or infrastructure which will be funded through the levy.

If a designated LPA already has an adopted CIL in place, then the process will not change for the purpose of applications submitted under Section 62A. All applicants applying for permission will be required to submit a CIL Liability Form with their application made to the Planning Inspectorate. This will be required for registration and validation purposes. If exemption or relief is sought then the requisite form will also need to be submitted to the Planning Inspectorate.

In cases where planning permission is granted under Section 62A, a Liability Notice will be issued directly from the charging authority to the applicant following the grant of permission. This will set out the necessary payment information and schedule.

Although the application will be determined by a person appointed by the Secretary of State under Section 62A, the requirement for a CIL payment to the local authority and the subsequent charging process will be unaffected.

Planning Obligations

Planning obligations, also referred to as Section 106 Agreements, mitigate the impact of unacceptable development to make it acceptable in planning terms. The obligation is a formal legal agreement which is entered as a local land charge. It may place a restriction on the use of the land, require development to be carried out in a specific way, or require a financial contribution to be made by the applicant or their successor(s) in title.

Planning obligations may also be required even where a designated authority has adopted a CIL. However, in such cases, the Section 106 agreement should not contribute to wider projects included in the CIL Regulation 123 list.

How and when will I know if a planning obligation is required?

The Planning Inspectorate strongly advises all applicants pursuing applications through Section 62A to engage in pre-application discussions with all relevant stakeholders. This allows for a consideration of the main issues early in the development process where the likelihood of potential planning obligations can be raised. Agreement on the principles of a planning obligation should be reached with the local planning authority and any relevant stakeholders prior to submission.

During pre-application discussions the Planning Inspectorate will expect designated authorities to identify any tariffs that are already in place which may be triggered by the development proposal. It will be necessary for the LPA to justify the site specific need for a planning obligation.

In cases where no pre-application consultation has been carried out with the Planning Inspectorate, levy rates and/or relevant local policies should be provided with the Section 62A application questionnaire. By the end of the formal representation period, the Planning Inspectorate will expect all requests for planning obligations to have been made formally in writing, including an appropriate level of information and detailed justification. Where this has not been provided, the late submission of requests for planning obligations will only be accepted at the discretion of the appointed person.

Who is responsible for progressing an obligation?

Where a planning obligation is proposed the Planning Inspectorate will not be a signatory. The agreement will remain between the applicant, the landowner(s), and the LPA. The designation of an LPA for the purpose of Section 62A applications does not change this. Consequently, the Planning Inspectorate will not be responsible for the preparation or completion of any agreements. When considering if an LPA should be de-designated, its performance on progressing legal agreements is one of the factors that are taken into account.

It is the responsibility of the applicant and the LPA to ensure that a completed agreement is in place so that it can be taken into account in reaching a decision. For applications dealt with by the written representations procedure, the completed agreement should be submitted within two weeks of the end of the representation period. Where there is a hearing, and the proposal is for major development, the agreement should be received one week prior to the hearing date. Where the proposal is for non-major development the agreement should be received in advance of the hearing.

For the obligation to be considered complete, it must be signed and dated by all relevant parties and accompanied by a plan where necessary. A certified copy should be submitted to the Planning Inspectorate accompanied by a declaration from the LPA certifying that that it is a true copy of the original. The LPA should retain the original copy.

Where there is disagreement between the parties as to the content of an agreement, or whether all aspects are necessary, it can be structured so that the appointed person can direct that certain obligations are ‘struck out’ in the event they are found to fail the relevant tests. Where such an approach is taken, the applicant must submit a statement detailing reasons for each obligation they believe does not meet the tests. The LPA should set out its case in its formal representation.

Where the applicant considers that financial contributions would render the scheme unviable, the Planning Inspectorate will expect them to submit site-specific information relating to the viability of the scheme, to be submitted with the application. Where detailed analysis of additional information is required, an extension of time agreement may be necessary.

What should be submitted with an application?

Applicants should also be aware that some designated LPAs may set out the need for a draft Heads of Terms or draft Unilateral Undertaking as part of their local validation requirements. As a consequence, some applications will need to be submitted with this information for validation purposes. Given the timescales involved, however, applicants are strongly encouraged to have any obligations at an advanced stage prior to the submission of the application.

Parties to the planning obligation

Normally, all persons with an interest in land affected by a planning obligation – including freeholder(s), leaseholder(s), holders of any estate contract(s) and any mortgagees – must sign the obligation. Where there are different ownerships it may be necessary to define them by reference to a plan. Where an obligation is proposed, the agreement will remain between the applicant and the LPA, the Planning Inspectorate will not be a signatory.

The planning obligation must give details of each person’s title to the land. This should be checked by the LPA and the appointed person will ask for its assurance. Normally this is in the form of an up-to-date copy of an entry or entries from the Land Registry.

Where an applicant has only an option to purchase the land, the current landowner(s) will need to be party to any obligation binding the land. Counterpart documents are legal documents identical in all respects except that each is signed by a different party or parties. This is not appropriate in the case of planning obligations, since these are public law documents which are entered on the planning register and the local land charges register and are often copied to residents and other interested people. The planning obligation should be one single document executed by all the relevant parties.

There may be exceptional circumstances where it is agreed in advance by the parties that counterparts are the only practical option. In these cases, both the appointed person and the LPA should be satisfied that certified copies of all of the individually signed documents have been provided (by a solicitor or other suitably legally qualified person).

Format of the planning obligation

Where designated local authorities use a standard format for planning obligations, then these should continue to be used through the Section 62A process.

At a minimum it is expected that all parts of a planning obligation, including the signatures, should follow in sequence without gaps. The signatures should not normally start on a new page.

Any manuscript alterations to the text must be initialled by all the parties. Any documents or plans which are annexed to the planning obligation must be clearly identified in the text (by document title and date or drawing number) and any plans which are identified must be attached. Any plans must be signed by all the parties and any colouring of plans must match the description given in the text. If any plan is found to be inaccurate or missing, the planning obligation will need to be re-executed with the correct plan(s) attached.

Content of the planning obligation

In summary, a planning obligation or Section 106 agreement should include the following:

  • the land to which it relates (by a plan if necessary);
  • the parties to the obligation, by names and addresses, and their relevant interest in the land. If a party is an offshore company it must give an address for service of documents in the UK;
  • that it is a planning obligation and name the LPA by which it is enforceable;
  • that it comes into effect upon the grant of planning permission - even if the actions required by the obligation are triggered by subsequent events, such as commencement of the development;
  • precisely the requirements which it imposes on the party or parties giving the covenant(s) in sufficient detail (including the parts of the land to which they are to apply, where relevant) to make them enforceable; and
  • that any financial contributions are to be paid to the local planning authority or (by a suitably worded provision in the deed) any other relevant authority responsible for the provision of the particular public services to which the contributions apply.

It might be necessary to define, by reference to a plan, the proposed site(s) of particular facilities (e.g. open space) to be provided, or the detailed specification of the purposes to which particular financial contributions are to be put (including any time limits, quality checks, etc. which are to be applied).

It must make it clear when each of its requirements is triggered and whether there are any conditions affecting the performance of that requirement. For example, it should make it clear whether some other event needs to occur, or formal notice needs to be given, before a financial contribution becomes payable; or whether the terms of a transfer of land need to be agreed before affordable housing or some other community benefit is delivered.

Can a Unilateral Undertaking be submitted?

Where an applicant is already aware of the need for a planning obligation, or where an applicant considers a particular obligation to be sufficient to meet the relevant tests then a unilateral undertaking can be submitted. Where this is the case, the Planning Inspectorate will expect the undertaking to be submitted with the planning application.

Where a unilateral undertaking is submitted as part of the planning application, it will be for the applicant to provide the necessary information to demonstrate why it is sufficient. Unless this is revised during the course of the application the appointed person will proceed on the basis of the information submitted.

A unilateral undertaking should not try to impose requirements or obligations on any person other than the signing party e.g. it would not be acceptable to try to require a Registered Provider to exchange contracts within a set period.

Planning obligations and the provision of affordable housing

If a planning obligation provides for affordable housing as part of the proposed development, the Planning Inspectorate will need to be satisfied that:

  • the type(s) of affordable housing which it is proposed to provide are satisfactorily defined;
  • where there is a split between the different types of affordable housing it is justified and that there are arrangements to secure it;
  • there are clear and specific provisions dealing with the distribution of the affordable housing;
  • the covenants are drafted in a way which will ensure delivery of the proposed housing. The planning obligation should state who is to be responsible for the construction of the affordable housing;
  • if the land to be used for affordable housing is to be transferred (e.g. to a Registered Provider), the relevant land is clearly identified on a plan, and there is a restriction on development until arrangements for the transfer are made as set out in the planning obligation or in a document annexed to it;
  • if the Registered Provider is a party to the planning obligation, it includes positive covenants to ensure that the affordable housing will be constructed and (by a suitably worded provision) transferred to the Registered Provider (possibly with a cascading mechanism in case of default by the preferred Registered Provider);
  • if none of the parties to the planning obligation is a Registered Provider (and assuming the applicant itself is not going to build the affordable housing), there are adequate and reasonable arrangements for securing a Registered Provider;
  • the phasing arrangements for delivery of the affordable housing are satisfactory. The planning obligation should not allow most of the market housing to be sold before the affordable units are available for occupation. The provision/occupation of both types of housing should be appropriately synchronised;
  • if the affordable housing is to be provided off-site, or a financial contribution made in lieu of provision, there is robust justification for this, and what is on offer is of broadly equivalent value (see paragraph 50 of the Framework);
  • the planning obligation contains adequate controls to ensure that any affordable housing is retained as affordable for an unlimited duration;
  • the arrangements for allocating the affordable housing (e.g. nomination rights involving use of the local authority’s housing waiting list or allocations to qualifying persons by a Registered Provider) are satisfactory;
  • if the planning obligation includes a cascade arrangement, there are adequate time-periods at each stage, especially before triggering any “fall-back” clause which would enable the affordable housing to revert to the developer for sale on the open market; and
  • the proposed arrangements for managing the affordable housing are adequate.
