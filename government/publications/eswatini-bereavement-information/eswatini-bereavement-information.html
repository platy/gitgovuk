2. Introduction

When a relative or friend dies abroad, the different procedures, laws or language can cause additional distress. You may be uncertain about what to do or who to contact.

This country specific information is designed to help you through some of the practical arrangements you may need to make. It supplements the general information on death abroad produced by the Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office, which applies to all countries.

Please note, as each country has its own laws and customs when a death occurs, it may not be possible to make the arrangements that you prefer, or at the time you would like.

3.  How to contact the Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office

There is a lot of information below, but you may have questions. You can speak to someone by phone 24/7 any day of the year by contacting the Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office in London on 0207 008 1500.

If you are not in the UK, this is where you can find the contact details of the nearest British embassy, high commission or consulate online.

The priority of the Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office is to provide assistance to British nationals overseas who need the most help. The level and type of assistance they can offer is tailored to the individual circumstances of each case.

4. Next of kin

The next of kin of the person who died will usually need to make decisions and practical arrangements. The next of kin can sometimes appoint another person to act on their behalf.

If you are not the next of kin, they will need to be informed. If required, the Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office can help you do this.

There is no legal definition of next of kin in the UK. In Eswatini, next of kin may be any individual that has been chosen to represent an individual. This can be a spouse, other relative or any person with power of attorney.

Please note that same sex marriages and relationships are illegal in Eswatini, so the government may not recognise a same-sex partner as next of kin.

If the next of kin is abroad and unable to travel the police normally use photographic identification to identify the body visually.

Photographic ID/ passport can be used if found near or adjacent to identify a body damaged beyond recognition. In the absence of photographic identification, dental records, fingerprints or DNA can be used to confirm the identity of a deceased person. According to local authorities, Eswatini has labs that deal with such cases and authorities have told us the turnaround time is 7 days at most.

If there is a disagreement over who is the next of kin, or the person who died did not choose a next of kin, this can cause additional complications. If there is a dispute in the family over the next of kin, courts will decide over the dispute.

When someone dies, the next of kin in Eswatini or abroad normally notifies local authorities.

5. Release of information to next of kin

The Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office will try to obtain as much information as possible after your relative or friend has died abroad. Some of this may only be available to the next of kin.

Consular officers may be able to obtain this themselves, or they may put you in touch directly with the authorities overseas. They may be able to provide you with details of others who can advocate on your behalf such as lawyers, charities, or other organisations.

There are no local laws or standard procedures relating to the releasing of information to the family. The local authorities normally release information to the next of kin or family. It is unusual for information to be withheld by local authorities.

The appointed funeral director, next of kin or High Commission usually keep the family informed of any developments.

6. Appointing a funeral director

If the person who died did not have insurance, the next of kin will usually have to appoint a funeral director and will usually be responsible for all costs. The Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office cannot help with these costs.

The next of kin or relative must identify the body. A relative or a next of kin must instruct a local funeral director in Eswatini or an international funeral director in the UK for a body to be repatriated to the UK, or buried or cremated in Eswatini.

However, if the deceased was insured, you should immediately contact the insurance company to establish if they are able to cover for the repatriation expenses and make the necessary arrangements.

You may need the insurance policy number and the associated 24-hour medical emergency contact number to do this. If the travel insurance company confirms that there is a current policy, you should not appoint your own funeral director or be pressurised by local funeral agencies to do so. It will be the insurance company that will appoint the funeral director both locally and in the UK.

If you decide to bring the deceased to the UK for the funeral or cremation, you may only need to appoint an international funeral director. The Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office produces a list of international funeral directors based in the UK).

If you decide to hold a funeral or cremation in Eswatini, you can find a list of local funeral directors online.

7. Registering the death and obtaining a death certificate

You will need to register the death with the local authorities in the country where the person died. You will usually need documents about you and the person who has died, which include information such as full name, date of birth and passport number.

When someone dies in Eswatini, you do not need to register the death with the UK authorities. The local death certificate can usually be used in the UK for most purposes, including probate. In Eswatini, funeral directors cannot register death on behalf of the next of kin. Authorities have told us registration can be done over email if the next of kin is in the UK and unable to travel to Eswatini.

In Eswatini, a death must be registered at the Ministry of Home Affairs offices across the country within 60 days. A doctor who confirms a person has died will provide a certification of cause of death (BMD 10). When someone dies in the villages at home, a chief from the local council writes a letter and it can be used as a BMD 10.

Note that these documents are required in order to formally register the death and receive an official death certificate:

  • BMD 10 certification of cause of death (if the person died at a health facility); or a letter from Umphakatsi (local council), with an official stamp, where the deceased was resident (if the person died at home)
  • deceased’s birth certificate
  • identity card of the deceased
  • identity card of the person registering the death
  • a document (i.e. a birth or marriage certificate) that links the deceased with the informant

If the death did not occur at home or at a health facility, further documentation may be needed, such a as a police report.

In case of late registration, a requesting affidavit or sworn statement is required. This means the next of kin must write a statement under oath detailing the reasons for late registration. You will need to obtain this from a notary public. A list of lawyers in Eswatini can be found here.

8. Post mortem examinations (autopsies)

A post mortem is required in all cases where a person has died in Eswatini and is being repatriated abroad, and in most cases of unnatural or unknown causes of death. The decision to conduct a post mortem is usually made by the police.

Post mortems are conducted on demand (within 1-2 days) by a State Pathologist working on behalf of the Ministry of Justice. The pathologists have the capabilities to perform laboratory testing and toxicology in the local forensic lab.

Families are not charged for post mortems and organs are not retained after post mortem examinations. A next of kin can request a preliminary post mortem report from the Royal Eswatini Police and a private post mortem conducted by a private doctor. There are costs involved if the family decides to conduct a private post mortem.

9. Mortuary facilities

Eswatini mortuary facilities may not meet the same standard that you would experience in the UK. Mortuaries are usually attached to local hospitals and private funeral parlours. There are dedicated state mortuary facilities.

The cold storage facilities are available throughout the country. There is a daily storage cost for keeping a body in the mortuary.

10. Burial, cremation, repatriation

The next of kin of the person who has died will usually need to decide between a local burial, cremation or bringing the person home, which is known as repatriation. Your funeral director will usually be able to explain the options available, the costs, and help you make arrangements.

Local burial and local cremation is allowed for foreigners. Burials and cremations will only take place once a death certificate has been obtained from the Ministry of Home Affairs. A body cannot be donated for medical science. There are no regulations to local scatterings of ashes.

10.1 Maximum period before Interment

In Eswatini, there is no prescribed period within which burial must take place. This is provided that the remains are not a health risk and are kept in a place of cold storage, approved by the medical officer of the Ministry of Health. The alternative would be to have the remains embalmed or sealed in a hermetically-sealed container, as approved by a medical officer of the Ministry of Health.

If death results from unnatural causes (assault, auto accident, etc.), it is the responsibility of the police to transport the body to the government morgue for post mortem examination. In such instances, the remains may not be removed prior to completion of the examination, which generally is performed on request within 1-2 days.

10.2 Embalming

There is no prescribed period within which embalming must take place, provided the remains are not a health risk and are kept in a place of cold storage approved by the medical officer of the Ministry of Health.  Local burial does not require embalming and burials can be arranged through the municipal authorities. This is usually arranged by the funeral directors.

10.3 Repatriation

The funeral director is responsible for coordinating the following documents required to repatriate the body of someone who has died.

International requirements for repatriations are as follows:

  • passport of the person who died
  • Eswatini Removal Permit
  • death certificate
  • Embalming Certificate
  • Family Authorisation Letter
  • “No Contagious Diseases” Letter from a pathologist
  • medical report (cause of death)
  • health letter from the South African High Commission
  • BI 1663 form from the South African High Commission
  • authority to import remains into the US (Consular Mortuary Certificate)
  • transit visa through South Africa to OR Tambo Airport

The outer box containing the remains must be clearly marked to show the name of the deceased, the route to be taken, and “certified free of communicable diseases” notice. The remains must be sealed in a zinc-lined coffin.

Local requirements for repatriation are as follows:

  • passports and permits
  • Embalming certificate
  • a death certificate issued by the District Registrar
  • a statement from the forensic pathologist to the effect that the body contains no infections or contagious diseases
  • a permit for the removal of a body issued by the Permanent Secretary’s office in the Ministry of Health
  • a statement from the Ministry of Health to the effect that the deceased did not reside in an epidemic area
  • letter from the deceased family confirming that they will meet/receive the body on arrival at the airport, or providing the complete contact details of their agent

There are no direct flights from Eswatini to the United Kingdom. Most bodies that are returned to the UK will travel via South Africa.

Additional requirements to ship a body through South Africa:

  • the body must be embalmed, placed in a hermetically sealed zinc-lined casket within a hardwood coffin enclosed in a packing case
  • a certificate (affidavit) by the embalmer attesting that the body has been embalmed
  • an affidavit by the mortician who closed the casket
  • a statement by a doctor certifying that the body is non-infectious
  • a letter of conveyance issued by the High Commission of the Republic of South Africa in Eswatini

10.4 Cremation

Dups Funeral Home and Crematorium is the only mortuary in Eswatini to offer cremation services. They are based in Manzini.

The following documents are needed before conducting a cremation:

  • death certificate
  • Schedule A Forms (Supplied by Dups to be completed by the next of kin)
  • Schedule B Forms (Supplied by Dups to be completed by Medical Practitioner)

If you wish to return the ashes to the UK, Dups will need to provide a cremation certificate for you to present to the airline.

Please note if a local burial or cremation takes place, then an inquest in the UK will not be possible. For more information on inquests, see the information on UK coroners and inquests below.

11. Return of personal belongings

If the person who died had insurance, check with the insurance company if this covers the return of their personal belongings.

Please note, the British High Commission or Consulate cannot take responsibility for the personal belongings of the person who died.

In Eswatini, a request to store personal belongings must be made through the Ministry of Home Affairs and or Ministry of Foreign Affairs. Personal belongings are removed from the body and handed over to the next of kin.

Funeral directors are not responsible for returning personal belongings to the United Kingdom and there is no mechanism in place for returning personal belongings.

12. Steps to take in the UK

You can find more information on the steps to take in the UK online. This includes information on arranging the funeral, telling the government about the death, UK pensions and benefits, and dealing with the estate of the person who died. There is a step-by-step guide about what to do when someone dies.

13. British passport cancellation

In order to avoid identity fraud, the passport of the person who died should be cancelled with Her Majesty’s Passport Office (HMPO). To do this, you will need to complete a “D1 form”.

The form and instructions on where to send it is available online.

If you plan to repatriate the person who died to the UK, you may require their passport to do this. In these circumstances, you should cancel the passport after they have been repatriated.

14. Child deaths

Police in Eswatini will hold a full investigation into any unnatural deaths and there are no separate procedures for any kind of deaths involving children.

15. Deaths in road traffic accidents

Police in Eswatini will hold a full investigation into any unnatural deaths and there are no separate procedures for deaths involving road traffic accidents.

16. Deaths investigated as murder or manslaughter

If the local police have confirmed that they are investigating the death as a murder or manslaughter a dedicated team within the Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office will be available to provide support, including by referring you to a specialised organisation. You can find more about what they can do online.

You should note that if the deceased is repatriated to parts of the UK, a coroner or procurator fiscal may decide to hold an inquest. See the section on UK Coroners and inquests below.

If a death is regarded as suspicious the Royal Eswatini Police will investigate in full. If an offender is identified and charged, they will be prosecuted by the relevant Director of Public Prosecutions.

17. UK coroners and inquests

{:#coroners-inquests)

If you repatriate the person who died to England and Wales, there may be an inquest. The decision on when to hold an inquest is made by Her Majesty’s Coroner.

Please note, an inquest will usually only happen in certain situations, for example, when someone has died in suspicious, unnatural, and violent circumstances or whilst in detention. If the person who died is cremated and only their ashes are brought home, there will not be an inquest.

If you repatriate the person who died to Scotland, the Procurator Fiscal may decide to call for a Fatal Accidents or Injuries Inquiry.

If you repatriate the person who died to Northern Ireland, there will be no coronial inquest or further inquiry.

Please note, Procurators Fiscal and Coroners do not have jurisdiction in another country, nor do they seek to apportion blame to a named individual.

You can find more information on Coroners and the Procurator Fiscal in the FCDO guide on death abroad

18. Legal Aid

The government only provides legal assistance to suspects of crimes, for which the death penalty can be imposed. Some NGOs provide legal assistance on an ad hoc basis. Not allowing legal representation in criminal cases at Swazi National Courts is contrary to international standards.

19. Compensation

There is no compensation scheme for victims of murder, manslaughter or terrorism in Eswatini. You can find information on UK compensation for victims of terrorism overseas online.

20. Support organisations in the UK

In the UK, there are many organisations that can help bereaved families. Some of these are listed in the guide for coping with death abroad. We also have information for victims of crime abroad, which you may find helpful.
